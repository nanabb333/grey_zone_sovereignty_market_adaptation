# Research Question V2

## Original Research Question

How does geopolitical risk affect Taiwan financial markets?

## Problem with the Original Question

The original question is useful but too narrow for the current empirical patterns.

The findings show that:

1. Risk events are negative on average but not uniformly negative.
2. Strategic_Importance events are positive on average but not uniformly positive.
3. Some military events generate positive abnormal returns.
4. Some technology events generate negative abnormal returns.
5. Market reactions appear to vary across TWSE and TSMC.

These patterns suggest that the project should not treat geopolitical risk as a single direct input into market reaction.

## Revised Main Research Question

How do Taiwan-related geopolitical events affect financial markets through competing investor interpretations of threat, strategic importance, surprise, and adaptation?

## Revised Sub-Questions

1. When are Taiwan-related events interpreted as geopolitical threats rather than strategic-importance signals?
2. Do Risk events produce more negative abnormal returns than Strategic_Importance events?
3. Do repeated grey-zone events generate weaker or less negative market reactions over time?
4. Do more surprising events produce larger abnormal returns than anticipated events?
5. Do TWSE and TSMC respond differently to the same event because they reflect different exposures to Taiwan risk and semiconductor opportunity?

## Revised Hypotheses for Future Testing

These are proposed for future empirical testing and should not be treated as established findings.

| hypothesis | statement |
| --- | --- |
| H1 | Events interpreted as threats are associated with more negative abnormal returns. |
| H2 | Events interpreted as strategic-importance signals are associated with more positive abnormal returns. |
| H3 | Higher event repetition is associated with weaker or less negative abnormal returns. |
| H4 | Higher surprise is associated with larger absolute abnormal returns. |
| H5 | TSMC reactions differ from TWSE reactions when events contain semiconductor or supply-chain content. |

## Revised Project Framing

The project should now be framed around grey-zone sovereignty as a dual market condition.

Grey-zone sovereignty creates:

- vulnerability to military, diplomatic, and coercive risk;
- strategic value from Taiwan's role in semiconductors, AI, and U.S.-China technology competition;
- adaptation as investors learn repeated grey-zone patterns;
- surprise when events deviate from expected patterns.

The revised research question better captures this dual structure.
