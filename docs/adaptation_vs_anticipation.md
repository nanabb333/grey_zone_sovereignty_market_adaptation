# Adaptation vs Anticipation

> Academic Research Notice:
> This document is provided for educational and research reference purposes only.
> It contains a methodological clarification for dissertation revision.
> It should not be treated as a causal identification result.

## Purpose

This memo addresses a central rival explanation in the dissertation: whether muted or positive market reactions to repeated Taiwan-related shocks reflect investor adaptation, or whether they instead reflect anticipation and prior pricing.

The dissertation's current framework is:

```text
Grey-Zone Sovereignty
-> Recurring Taiwan-Related Shocks
-> Investor Interpretation
-> Market Reaction
```

The central mechanism is investor adaptation. However, the current evidence observes market reactions rather than investor beliefs directly. For that reason, the adaptation claim should be presented as an interpretation that is consistent with the evidence, not as a fully identified causal mechanism.

## 1. Why Adaptation and Anticipation Are Different

Adaptation and anticipation can both reduce the apparent market damage of geopolitical shocks, but they imply different mechanisms.

### Adaptation

Adaptation refers to investor learning across repeated shocks. Under an adaptation interpretation, investors observe recurring Taiwan-related events, update their understanding of escalation risk, and increasingly distinguish between symbolic coercion, contained military signaling, and genuinely destabilizing escalation. The same objective event type may therefore produce weaker or more conditional market reactions over time because investor interpretation changes.

In this project, adaptation is most relevant to repeated grey-zone events such as PLA exercises and Joint Sword episodes. The logic is not that risk disappears. Rather, the market may learn to price recurring coercive signaling as part of Taiwan's standing grey-zone environment rather than as evidence of imminent conflict.

### Anticipation

Anticipation refers to prior pricing before the event date. Under an anticipation interpretation, investors expect the event before Day 0 and adjust prices before the event window or before the formal event date used in the study. The measured Day-0 or post-event CAR may therefore appear muted, not because investors have adapted to a recurring risk environment, but because the market already incorporated the expected information.

Anticipation is especially relevant when official warnings, media reports, diplomatic schedules, military preparations, or prior retaliatory patterns make the event foreseeable.

## 2. Why Both Mechanisms Can Generate Similar CAR Patterns

Adaptation and anticipation may produce similar observed CAR patterns:

| Observed Pattern | Adaptation Interpretation | Anticipation Interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Weak Day-0 reaction | Investors interpret the event as familiar or contained. | Prices adjusted before Day 0. |
| Positive post-event CAR | Investors view the event as less damaging than feared or as routine signaling. | The event was already priced in, and uncertainty is resolved after occurrence. |
| Smaller reaction to later repeated events | Markets learn from prior episodes and downgrade shock severity. | Later events are more predictable because prior episodes reveal the likely response pattern. |
| Stronger reaction to novel events | New information changes interpretation. | New information was not priced before the event. |

This overlap is important. Similar CAR evidence cannot, by itself, distinguish adaptation from anticipation. Both mechanisms operate through investor expectations and interpretation, and both can reduce measured event-window damage.

## 3. Why Current Evidence Is Only Consistent With Adaptation

The current evidence is consistent with adaptation because repeated Taiwan-related military demonstrations do not uniformly produce negative abnormal returns, and later Joint Sword episodes appear less damaging than a simple risk-damage model would predict. This supports the dissertation's conditional interpretation of geopolitical risk.

However, the evidence does not directly observe investor learning. It also does not fully establish whether prices moved before the event window. Therefore, the dissertation should avoid claiming that adaptation is proven. A more defensible formulation is:

```text
The observed pattern is consistent with market adaptation, but it may also reflect anticipation, prior pricing, or broader market conditions. The evidence supports a conditional interpretation of geopolitical risk rather than an automatic risk-damage model.
```

This framing strengthens the dissertation because it explicitly acknowledges the strongest rival explanation while preserving the theoretical contribution.

## 4. What Evidence Would Strengthen Adaptation Claims

The adaptation interpretation would be stronger if future evidence showed:

1. Repeated event families produce weaker abnormal returns over time after controlling for benchmarks.
2. Similar event types become less negative even when pre-event media coverage is limited.
3. Later events show reduced volatility or shorter recovery periods compared with earlier comparable events.
4. Investor commentary, analyst reports, or financial media describe later events as routine, expected, or contained.
5. Event-family repetition predicts weaker market reactions even after accounting for surprise level.
6. The market reacts more strongly to genuinely novel Taiwan-related shocks than to repeated grey-zone events.

These forms of evidence would not eliminate all rival explanations, but they would make the adaptation mechanism more credible.

## 5. What Evidence Would Falsify Adaptation Claims

The adaptation interpretation would be weakened or falsified if:

1. Muted CARs occur only when events are heavily anticipated before Day 0.
2. Repeated-event effects disappear after coding surprise and prior media coverage.
3. Earlier and later events in the same family produce similar reactions once benchmark controls are applied.
4. Positive returns are fully explained by global technology or semiconductor-sector momentum.
5. Investor commentary frames later events as already priced rather than as routine or less threatening.
6. New repeated grey-zone events still produce large negative abnormal returns despite high repetition and low surprise.

In these cases, the dissertation should shift from an adaptation-centered interpretation toward a broader expectations framework in which adaptation is one possible channel, but not the dominant explanation.

## Dissertation Implication

The strongest version of the argument is not that adaptation has been conclusively identified. It is that Taiwan-related geopolitical risk is mediated by investor interpretation, and that adaptation is a plausible mechanism within that interpretive process. Anticipation should be treated as a rival mechanism that can be partially addressed through surprise coding, pre-event media evidence, and event-family repetition measures.
