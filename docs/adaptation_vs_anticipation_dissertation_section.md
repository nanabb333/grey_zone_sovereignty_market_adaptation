# Adaptation vs Anticipation: Dissertation-Ready Section

> Academic Research Notice:
> This document provides dissertation-ready prose for integration into the discussion chapter.
> It is written as interpretive text and does not claim causal identification.

## Proposed Section Text

A central interpretive challenge in this dissertation is distinguishing adaptation from anticipation. The empirical results show that some repeated Taiwan-related risk events, especially later military-demonstration events, do not generate the uniformly negative abnormal returns predicted by a simple risk-damage model. This pattern is consistent with investor adaptation, but it can also be explained by anticipation or prior pricing. The distinction matters because both mechanisms can produce similar event-study patterns while implying different theoretical processes.

Adaptation refers to investor learning across repeated shocks. Under an adaptation interpretation, investors observe recurring Taiwan-related events and gradually refine how they interpret them. A major diplomatic shock or military exercise may initially be read as a signal of acute escalation risk. Over time, however, repeated episodes may allow investors to distinguish between events that materially change expected fundamentals and events that belong to an established repertoire of grey-zone signaling. Adaptation does not mean that investors ignore geopolitical risk. It means that repeated exposure may change how risk is classified, weighted, and priced. Within the dissertation's framework, adaptation operates through investor interpretation: recurring shocks may become less novel, less ambiguous, or more easily categorized as contained.

Anticipation is different. Anticipation refers to the possibility that investors expected the event before Day 0 and therefore adjusted prices before the event window used in the study. Many Taiwan-related events are not complete surprises. High-level visits may be discussed before they occur. Inaugurations and political speeches are scheduled. PRC military responses may be expected after prominent U.S.-Taiwan interactions or sovereignty-related statements. If investors already expect a response, then the formal event date may contain limited new information. A muted or positive CAR around Day 0 may therefore reflect prior pricing rather than adaptation.

This rival explanation is especially important because adaptation and anticipation overlap empirically. Repeated events are often easier to anticipate. If investors have observed previous PRC military responses, they may both understand the event family better and expect future responses more readily. As a result, later Joint Sword episodes may appear less damaging either because investors have adapted to recurring grey-zone coercion or because the market already priced the likely response before the measured event window. The current event-study evidence cannot fully separate these mechanisms.

The introduction of `surprise_score`, `event_family`, and `event_repetition_level` helps clarify this problem. `surprise_score` indicates whether an event was likely to introduce new information on or near Day 0. `event_family` identifies which events are substantively comparable. `event_repetition_level` captures whether an event belongs to a recurring pattern. These variables make it possible to ask whether muted market reactions are concentrated among low-surprise events, highly repeated event families, or both. If muted reactions occur mainly among low-surprise events, anticipation is a strong rival explanation. If muted reactions persist among repeated events that are not clearly low surprise, the adaptation interpretation becomes more plausible.

The current findings should therefore be stated carefully. They are consistent with adaptation, but they do not prove adaptation. Joint Sword-2024B is the clearest example. It is a risk event because it involved military activity around Taiwan, yet it is associated with positive abnormal returns in the main results. This is difficult for a simple risk-damage model to explain. However, the event also belongs to a repeated military-exercise family and may have been partly anticipated. Its market reaction may reflect adaptation, anticipation, broader semiconductor-sector conditions, or some combination of these forces.

The most defensible interpretation is that Taiwan-related geopolitical risk is filtered through investor expectations. Adaptation is one plausible form of expectation formation: investors learn from repeated shocks and adjust the meaning they assign to later events. Anticipation is another: investors price expected events before the event date. Both mechanisms support the dissertation's broader claim that market reactions are mediated by interpretation rather than determined mechanically by objective risk. The evidence therefore strengthens a conditional theory of geopolitical risk, even while leaving the specific adaptation mechanism provisional.

This caution improves rather than weakens the dissertation. Acknowledging anticipation, prior pricing, and information leakage prevents the argument from overstating what an exploratory event study can identify. The dissertation does not need to prove that adaptation is the sole cause of muted market reactions. Its contribution is to show that the risk-to-market relationship is conditional, that repeated Taiwan-related shocks do not always produce automatic market damage, and that investor interpretation is central to understanding financial responses under grey-zone sovereignty.
