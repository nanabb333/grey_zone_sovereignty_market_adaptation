# Theory Framework

## Public Summary

The theory links Taiwan's grey-zone sovereignty position to recurring geopolitical shocks and conditional market reactions.

The core logic is:

```text
Grey-zone sovereignty
→ recurring Taiwan-related shocks
→ investor interpretation
→ market reaction
```

Taiwan's political status creates recurring geopolitical tension. Financial markets do not necessarily react mechanically to each event. Instead, reactions may depend on how investors interpret the event's novelty, severity, repetition, and economic meaning.

## Main Mechanisms

### Risk

Risk events are geopolitical shocks that may increase perceived uncertainty, escalation risk, or market stress.

Examples include diplomatic shocks, military demonstrations, economic coercion, and political signaling.

### Strategic Importance

Strategic-importance events may reinforce Taiwan's economic or technological value, especially through semiconductors, AI infrastructure, or supply-chain relevance.

These events may not behave like pure risk shocks because investors may interpret them as evidence of Taiwan's continued strategic value.

## Adaptation Claim

The project treats adaptation as a plausible interpretation, not a proven mechanism.

The findings are consistent with adaptation when repeated grey-zone events generate muted or less negative abnormal returns. However, the evidence does not directly observe investor beliefs or learning.

## Rival Explanation

Anticipation and prior pricing remain central rival explanations.

If investors expected an event before Day 0, the measured event-window reaction may be muted because prices already adjusted earlier.
