## Proposed Discussion Revision

The results of this dissertation support a conditional interpretation of Taiwan-related geopolitical risk. Risk events are negative on average, while strategic-importance events are positive on average. However, the pattern is not uniform. Some risk events generate muted or positive abnormal returns, and some strategic-importance events generate negative abnormal returns. These deviations are theoretically important because they challenge a simple model in which geopolitical risk automatically produces market damage.

The strongest interpretation is that the findings are consistent with investor adaptation, but they do not prove adaptation. Adaptation remains plausible because later repeated military-demonstration events appear less uniformly damaging than earlier crisis episodes. The sequence from the Pelosi Visit to Joint Sword 2023, Joint Sword-2024A, and Joint Sword-2024B suggests that repeated Taiwan-related shocks may become less novel over time. If investors learn to distinguish between recurring grey-zone signaling and events that materially change escalation risk, later shocks may generate weaker market reactions.

Yet adaptation is inferred rather than directly observed. The dissertation does not observe investor beliefs, analyst expectations, trading-desk behavior, or pre-event pricing decisions. It observes market prices around dated events and evaluates whether the pattern is consistent with the theoretical framework. For this reason, the results should not be described as proof that investors adapted to Taiwan-related geopolitical risk. They should be described as evidence that is compatible with adaptation and inconsistent with a purely automatic risk-damage model.

Anticipation is the most important rival explanation. Many Taiwan-related events are partly predictable. Diplomatic visits are often discussed in advance, inaugurations are scheduled, and PRC military responses may be expected after sovereignty-related political triggers. If investors expected an event before Day 0, then the relevant repricing may have occurred before the measured event window. In that case, a muted Day-0 reaction would reflect prior pricing rather than adaptation. Repeated events are especially difficult because they are both more familiar and more predictable.

The upgraded event coding helps clarify this problem. `surprise_score` identifies whether an event was likely to introduce new information near Day 0. `event_family` identifies comparable event types. `event_repetition_level` captures whether the event belongs to a recurring pattern. These variables do not solve the identification problem, but they make it more transparent. They allow the dissertation to distinguish among three related but different ideas: objective risk, expectedness, and repeated-event familiarity.

Joint Sword-2024B illustrates the interpretive challenge. It is classified as a risk event because it involved military activity around Taiwan. A simple risk model would expect a negative market response. Yet the event is associated with positive abnormal returns in the current results. This pattern is consistent with adaptation because Joint Sword-2024B belongs to a repeated military-exercise family and may have been interpreted as a familiar grey-zone signal. It is also consistent with anticipation because investors may have expected a PRC response before the formal event date. The case therefore strengthens the dissertation's conditional argument but does not isolate adaptation as the sole mechanism.

Strategic-importance events further support the interpretation framework. These events are positive on average because they may signal Taiwan's central role in semiconductor supply chains, allied industrial policy, and AI infrastructure. However, they are not automatically positive. BIS Advanced Semiconductor Export Controls shows that strategic importance can also be interpreted as restriction, fragmentation, or policy uncertainty. This reinforces the broader claim that market reactions depend on meaning, not merely on event labels.

The revised conclusion of the discussion is therefore cautious. Taiwan-related geopolitical shocks matter, but their market effects are mediated by investor interpretation. Adaptation is one plausible mechanism through which repeated shocks may become less damaging, but anticipation and prior pricing remain serious rival explanations. The dissertation's contribution is not to prove adaptation causally. It is to show that under grey-zone sovereignty, market responses to geopolitical risk are conditional, interpretive, and shaped by the interaction of threat, strategic importance, surprise, and repetition.

## Suggested Replacement Claim

Use:

```text
The findings are consistent with investor adaptation, especially in repeated military-demonstration events, but they do not prove adaptation. Anticipation and prior pricing remain plausible rival explanations.
```

Avoid:

```text
The findings prove that investors adapted to Taiwan-related geopolitical shocks.
```
