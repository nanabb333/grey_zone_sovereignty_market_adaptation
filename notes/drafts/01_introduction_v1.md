# Grey-Zone Sovereignty and Market Adaptation: Explaining Financial Responses to Taiwan-Related Geopolitical Shocks

Taiwan occupies one of the most consequential and ambiguous positions in contemporary international politics. It possesses many of the practical attributes normally associated with sovereignty: a democratically elected government, autonomous political institutions, an independent military, control over domestic economic policy, and deep integration into the global economy. Yet Taiwan lacks broad formal diplomatic recognition and remains structurally embedded within the strategic rivalry between the United States and the People's Republic of China. This combination produces a distinctive condition of grey-zone sovereignty: Taiwan is neither simply a recognized sovereign state in the conventional legal sense nor merely a passive object of great-power competition. It is a politically autonomous, economically indispensable, and diplomatically constrained actor whose status is continuously negotiated through military signaling, diplomatic practice, economic statecraft, and global supply-chain politics.

This ambiguous status has become increasingly important for financial markets. Taiwan is not only a site of geopolitical risk; it is also a central node in the global semiconductor economy. Its firms, especially Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company, occupy a pivotal role in advanced chip production, artificial intelligence infrastructure, and the technological foundations of U.S.-China competition. As a result, Taiwan-related events can carry multiple meanings for investors. A diplomatic visit may be interpreted as a crisis trigger. A military exercise may signal escalation risk. A semiconductor investment announcement may signal strategic value, policy support, or long-run supply-chain restructuring. The same geopolitical environment that generates vulnerability may also reinforce Taiwan's indispensability.

This paper begins from a puzzle in the relationship between geopolitical risk and financial markets. Conventional geopolitical-risk theories would lead us to expect that higher geopolitical risk should produce more negative market reactions. In this view, military escalation, diplomatic confrontation, coercive economic measures, and cross-strait tension should raise uncertainty and reduce expected asset values. Applied to Taiwan, this expectation appears intuitive. Taiwan faces persistent military pressure from China, limited diplomatic recognition, and recurrent episodes of crisis around elections, high-level diplomatic contact, and sovereignty-related political signaling. If geopolitical risk is priced primarily as danger, then Taiwan-related shocks should consistently damage Taiwan financial markets.

The preliminary event-study evidence does not fully conform to this expectation. Some Taiwan-related shocks do correspond to negative abnormal returns. The Pelosi Visit in August 2022 and Joint Sword 2023 are examples of events associated with negative market responses. These cases fit the conventional risk logic: a visible diplomatic or military escalation is followed by financial-market weakness after benchmark adjustment. Yet other cases complicate the pattern. Joint Sword-2024B, despite being a military demonstration, is associated with positive abnormal returns in the current event window. TSMC-related CHIPS Act and U.S. expansion events are also associated with positive abnormal returns. Strategic Importance events are positive on average, while Risk events are negative on average, but neither category is uniform. Some military events do not generate persistent market damage, and some technology-related events generate negative returns.

The central research puzzle is therefore not whether Taiwan-related geopolitical risk matters. It almost certainly does. The puzzle is why some Taiwan-related geopolitical shocks fail to generate persistent market damage despite elevated objective risk. Put differently, why do investors sometimes respond to Taiwan-related shocks as threats and at other times appear to price them as manageable, familiar, or even strategically valuable? This question matters because it challenges a simple model of geopolitical risk in which objective escalation maps directly onto negative financial outcomes. It suggests that market reactions may depend on interpretation as much as on the event itself.

The paper argues that Taiwan's grey-zone sovereignty generates two simultaneous forces: geopolitical risk and strategic importance. The first force is familiar. Taiwan's contested status exposes it to military pressure, diplomatic shocks, coercive economic measures, and recurrent uncertainty over cross-strait relations. These conditions can raise perceived risk and contribute to negative abnormal returns. The second force is less often integrated into market-based theories of geopolitical risk. Taiwan's contested status also magnifies its strategic value. Because Taiwan is central to advanced semiconductor production and U.S.-China technology competition, some events may highlight policy support, supply-chain resilience, industrial upgrading, or the strategic importance of firms such as TSMC. In those cases, a Taiwan-related event may not be interpreted only as danger. It may also be interpreted as evidence of Taiwan's continuing importance to global technology and security systems.

This argument requires an additional theoretical layer between geopolitical events and market reactions. The revised framework developed in this project places investor interpretation at the center of the analysis. Taiwan-related geopolitical events may be interpreted through at least four mechanisms: threat, opportunity, adaptation, and surprise. Threat refers to the perception that an event raises the probability of coercion, escalation, disruption, or conflict. Opportunity refers to the perception that an event highlights Taiwan's strategic value, policy support, or technological centrality. Adaptation refers to the possibility that repeated grey-zone events become more familiar over time, reducing the intensity of market reactions even when objective risk remains high. Surprise refers to the extent to which an event differs from prior expectations. A high-risk event that was widely anticipated may produce a weaker market reaction than a lower-risk event that changes investor expectations.

This interpretation-centered approach does not deny the importance of geopolitical risk. Rather, it treats risk as one component of a broader process through which markets interpret Taiwan's political and economic position. The empirical puzzle emerges precisely because Taiwan is not only a risk site. It is also a strategic asset within the global political economy. Grey-zone sovereignty therefore produces ambiguous market signals. The same event environment can generate fear of escalation, expectation of policy support, anticipation of repeated coercive behavior, or renewed recognition of Taiwan's supply-chain importance. Financial markets may respond differently depending on which interpretation dominates.

Methodologically, the paper uses an event-study design to examine Taiwan-related geopolitical events from 2022 through 2025. The analysis focuses on Taiwan's broader equity market, represented by the TWSE/TWSE Index, and on TSMC as a firm-level indicator of Taiwan's semiconductor and strategic-technology exposure. The event sample includes diplomatic shocks, military demonstrations, political signaling, economic coercion, export-control events, and semiconductor-policy announcements. Rather than relying only on raw returns, the project calculates benchmark-adjusted abnormal returns. TWSE returns are adjusted against a broader technology-market benchmark, while TSMC returns are adjusted against a semiconductor-sector benchmark. The analysis examines event-day abnormal returns and cumulative abnormal returns over short event windows.

The purpose of this method is not to claim that each event caused a specific market movement. Event-study designs are vulnerable to anticipation, overlapping event windows, benchmark choice, and broader market conditions. These limitations are especially important in the Taiwan case, where events often cluster around diplomatic visits, elections, speeches, and military responses. The purpose is more modest: to identify whether the observed pattern of abnormal returns is consistent with a simple risk model or whether it suggests a more complex relationship between geopolitical shocks and market interpretation. The current evidence supports the latter view. Risk events are negative on average, but not consistently. Strategic Importance events are positive on average, but not consistently. These patterns motivate a revised theoretical framework rather than a final causal conclusion.

The paper makes three contributions. First, it connects the concept of grey-zone sovereignty with financial-market behavior. Existing discussions of Taiwan's status often emphasize recognition, deterrence, military coercion, or great-power competition. This project extends that discussion by asking how markets price events that arise from Taiwan's ambiguous status. In doing so, it links International Relations theory with International Political Economy and financial event-study methods.

Second, the paper shows that geopolitical risk does not uniformly produce negative abnormal returns. The Taiwan case is especially useful because it combines visible military and diplomatic shocks with strategic economic value. The results indicate that market reactions vary across event types and assets. Risk events tend to be negative on average, but some risk events are positive. Strategic Importance events tend to be positive on average, but some technology-related events are negative. This variation is theoretically important because it suggests that objective risk alone is insufficient to explain financial responses.

Third, the paper develops a framework linking geopolitical shocks, investor interpretation, and market adaptation. The framework suggests that Taiwan-related events should be analyzed not only by their objective severity but also by how investors interpret them. A military event may be damaging if it is perceived as a new escalation, but less damaging if it resembles a familiar pattern of grey-zone signaling. A technology event may be positive if it signals policy support or strategic investment, but negative if it introduces restrictions, uncertainty, or supply-chain disruption. This framework provides a basis for future empirical work on surprise, repetition, event families, and asset-specific responses.

The broader significance of the project lies in its treatment of Taiwan as both a geopolitical flashpoint and a strategic economic node. In much public discourse, Taiwan is often framed either as a security problem or as a semiconductor powerhouse. Grey-zone sovereignty requires holding both dimensions together. Taiwan's political ambiguity creates risk, but its economic centrality creates value. Markets appear to respond to both. This duality helps explain why Taiwan-related shocks do not always generate the market damage that conventional geopolitical-risk expectations would predict.

The remainder of the paper develops this argument in stages. It first situates Taiwan's grey-zone sovereignty within debates on recognition, strategic ambiguity, and U.S.-China competition. It then describes the event-study design and the construction of the Taiwan-related event sample. The empirical sections present abnormal-return patterns for TWSE and TSMC, comparing Risk and Strategic Importance events across event windows. The discussion then evaluates market adaptation, competing explanations, and the limits of the current design. The paper concludes by proposing a revised research agenda for studying how financial markets interpret geopolitical shocks in cases where sovereignty, security, and strategic economic value are deeply intertwined.
