# Chapter 6: Discussion

## 6.1 Revisiting the Puzzle

This dissertation began from a puzzle: why do some Taiwan-related geopolitical shocks fail to generate persistent market damage despite elevated objective risk? Taiwan is a highly visible geopolitical flashpoint. Its contested sovereignty, exposure to Chinese military pressure, role in U.S.-China competition, and position in global semiconductor supply chains all suggest that Taiwan-related political shocks should matter for financial markets. The results in Chapter 5 support this general expectation, but they also complicate it.

The main empirical pattern is conditional rather than uniform. Geopolitical-risk events are negative on average. Strategic-importance events are positive on average. However, substantial variation exists within both categories. Some risk events are associated with positive abnormal returns, while one strategic-importance event, BIS Advanced Semiconductor Export Controls, is associated with negative abnormal returns. The key deviant case is Joint Sword-2024B: a military-demonstration event that would normally be expected to heighten geopolitical risk, yet is associated with positive abnormal returns in the main event window, especially for TSMC.

The purpose of this chapter is to evaluate possible explanations for this pattern. Chapter 5 described what happened. This chapter asks how the pattern should be interpreted. The analysis does not treat adaptation as established fact. Instead, it evaluates whether the results are consistent with an adaptation-based interpretation and compares that interpretation with plausible rivals. This distinction is important because the evidence is suggestive but not definitive. Market prices are observable; investor beliefs are not. Any discussion of adaptation must therefore remain inferential and cautious.

The central interpretive question is whether Taiwan's grey-zone sovereignty produces recurring shocks that investors gradually learn to price differently. The results are consistent with this possibility, but they do not exclude other explanations. Anticipation effects, semiconductor-cycle dynamics, AI-sector optimism, benchmark specification, and event-selection choices could all affect the observed abnormal returns. The contribution of this chapter is therefore not to declare a winner among explanations, but to clarify what each explanation can and cannot account for.

## 6.2 Evaluating the Adaptation Interpretation

The adaptation interpretation follows directly from the theoretical framework developed in Chapter 3. Grey-zone sovereignty is a persistent condition rather than a one-off crisis. Because Taiwan's status remains unresolved, Taiwan-related shocks recur. Military exercises, diplomatic protests, sovereignty-related speeches, U.S.-Taiwan interactions, and technology-policy events are repeated manifestations of the same underlying political ambiguity. If investors observe similar shocks over time, they may begin to distinguish between events that materially alter the risk environment and events that belong to a familiar repertoire of grey-zone signalling.

The most relevant empirical sequence is Pelosi Visit, Joint Sword 2023, Joint Sword-2024A, and Joint Sword-2024B. These events are not identical, but they form a recognisable sequence of Taiwan-related crisis or military-demonstration episodes. Pelosi Visit represents an earlier and more surprising diplomatic shock followed by major PRC pressure. Joint Sword 2023 followed the Tsai-McCarthy meeting and involved large-scale military exercises. Joint Sword-2024A followed Lai Ching-te's inauguration. Joint Sword-2024B followed another sovereignty-related political trigger and again involved military activity around Taiwan.

The sequence is compatible with adaptation because later events appear less uniformly negative than earlier ones. Pelosi Visit and Joint Sword 2023 are negative on average over the main event window. Joint Sword-2024A remains negative on average, but less strongly. Joint Sword-2024B is positive on average. The sequence should be interpreted as an illustrative pattern rather than a robust test of adaptation. It is not fully monotonic, and the number of observations is too small to support strong inference. Nevertheless, it is consistent with the possibility that repeated exposure reduced the novelty of later military demonstrations. If investors came to view some PLA exercises as recurring grey-zone signals rather than new escalatory breaks, their market response may have weakened.

Adaptation in this sense involves learning, expectation formation, and reduced novelty. Learning does not mean that investors believe geopolitical risk has disappeared. It means that investors may become better able to classify different types of Taiwan-related events. A novel diplomatic crisis may be priced as a larger threat because its implications are uncertain. A repeated military demonstration may be priced differently if it resembles earlier episodes and does not appear to change expected fundamentals. The same objective event type can therefore carry different informational content depending on its place in a sequence.

Joint Sword-2024B is important because it is the strongest challenge to a simple risk model. It is classified as a geopolitical-risk event because it was a military demonstration around Taiwan. Yet it appears among the most positive observations in the current results. This does not show that investors adapted. It does, however, provide a case where the adaptation interpretation has possible explanatory leverage. If a repeated military-demonstration event is not priced as damaging, one possible explanation is that investors interpreted it as a familiar, anticipated, or contained grey-zone signal.

The adaptation interpretation remains limited. First, adaptation is inferred rather than directly observed. The dissertation does not have survey evidence, analyst-expectation data, trading-desk evidence, or direct measures of investor beliefs. Second, the repetition sequence includes only four coded observations. Third, later events occurred in different market environments, especially during a period of strong technology-sector dynamics. Fourth, repeated events are also more likely to be anticipated, which makes adaptation difficult to separate from prior pricing. The correct conclusion is therefore cautious: the evidence is compatible with adaptation but cannot establish it as the only or necessarily dominant explanation.

## 6.3 Threat, Opportunity, and Investor Interpretation

Adaptation alone is insufficient to explain the results. The dissertation's framework does not argue that all market variation comes from repeated exposure. Rather, it argues that investor interpretation is the immediate mechanism linking events to market reactions. Adaptation changes interpretation over time, but it is not the only source of interpretation. Events may also be interpreted as threats or opportunities depending on their content.

Threat interpretation is the most direct channel for geopolitical-risk events. Military escalation, coercive economic measures, diplomatic shocks, and sovereignty-related political pressure can all raise perceived uncertainty. They may increase concerns about conflict risk, supply-chain disruption, investor confidence, or future policy instability. The negative average abnormal returns for risk events are consistent with this threat channel. The Pelosi crisis period, Joint Sword 2023, and Strait Thunder-2025A all fit the expectation that acute risk events can produce negative market responses.

The threat channel matters because it prevents the dissertation from overstating adaptation. If risk events were not negative on average, the project might imply that markets have largely discounted Taiwan-related geopolitical risk. The results do not support that claim. Objective risk remains important. Many events associated with military pressure or coercion are associated with negative abnormal returns. The puzzle is not that risk has no effect. The puzzle is that risk does not have a uniform effect.

Opportunity interpretation operates differently. Strategic-importance events may be interpreted as evidence of Taiwan's centrality to semiconductor supply chains, U.S. industrial policy, economic security, or long-run technology demand. CHIPS Act-related events and TSMC expansion announcements are associated with positive abnormal returns in the current sample. These events may signal state support, investment, supply-chain protection, or the strategic value of advanced semiconductor capacity. In these cases, Taiwan's geopolitical position may be read not only as vulnerability but also as economic significance.

However, opportunity interpretation is also conditional. BIS Advanced Semiconductor Export Controls is a strategic-importance event but is associated with negative abnormal returns. This suggests that strategic importance can generate either positive or negative interpretation depending on the event. Technology competition may imply policy support and investment, but it may also imply restriction, fragmentation, compliance costs, or uncertainty. Strategic importance is therefore not a synonym for market optimism.

The distinction between adaptation and interpretation is central. Interpretation is the immediate mechanism: investors price an event as threat, opportunity, constraint, familiar signal, or surprise. Adaptation is a deeper mechanism that may change interpretation over time. A repeated military event may still be threatening, but less threatening than a novel shock if investors view it as part of an established grey-zone pattern. A strategic-importance event may be positive if it signals support, but negative if it signals restriction. The results therefore point to a layered framework in which geopolitical risk and strategic importance provide the content of events, adaptation shapes how repeated events are read, and interpretation produces the market reaction.

This connects directly to Chapter 3. The theoretical framework proposed that Taiwan's grey-zone sovereignty generates recurring shocks, that repeated shocks may produce adaptation, and that investor interpretation mediates market response. The empirical results are compatible with this framework because they show average differences between risk and strategic-importance events, alongside deviant cases that require interpretation. Yet the framework remains provisional. It is a way to organise the patterns, not a definitive causal account.

## 6.4 Rival Explanations

### 6.4.1 Anticipation Effects

Anticipation is the most serious rival explanation for the adaptation interpretation. Many Taiwan-related events are not complete surprises. Diplomatic visits are often discussed in advance. Inaugurations and major speeches are scheduled. PRC responses to Taiwan-related political events may be expected. Military exercises following high-profile diplomatic or sovereignty-related triggers may therefore contain less new information than their objective severity suggests.

This explanation is plausible because event studies measure market movement around a defined window. If investors adjust before Day 0, then the observed event-window reaction may appear muted or positive even though the event affected expectations earlier. This is especially relevant for later Joint Sword events. If investors expected a PRC military response after Lai's inauguration or National Day speech, the response may have been partly priced before the formal military event date. In that case, weak or positive abnormal returns would not necessarily indicate adaptation. They could reflect prior pricing.

Anticipation can explain why repeated events may appear less damaging. Repetition makes events more predictable. If the market expects a familiar pattern of PRC signalling, the event date itself may contain little new information. Anticipation can also explain why surprise coding matters: Pelosi Visit appears more surprising than later Joint Sword events, and the later events may therefore produce weaker immediate reactions.

However, anticipation cannot fully explain the whole pattern by itself. It explains muted responses to expected risk events, but it does not explain why strategic-importance events are positive on average or why BIS export controls are negative. It also overlaps with adaptation rather than replacing it entirely. Adaptation concerns how repeated exposure changes the meaning of a class of events. Anticipation concerns whether a particular event was expected before Day 0. A repeated event can be both anticipated and interpreted as familiar. Future work must separate these mechanisms more clearly, but the present results cannot do so decisively.

### 6.4.2 Semiconductor-Cycle Dynamics

Semiconductor-cycle dynamics provide another plausible rival explanation, especially for TSMC. Semiconductor firms are affected by demand cycles, inventory adjustments, capital expenditure, utilisation rates, customer orders, and expectations about future technology demand. TSMC may move differently from the broader Taiwan market because of firm-specific or sector-specific conditions that are not primarily geopolitical.

This explanation is plausible because several of the strongest positive observations involve TSMC or semiconductor-related events. If TSMC was benefiting from favourable sector conditions, then positive abnormal returns around strategic-importance events may partly reflect industry fundamentals rather than investor interpretation of Taiwan's geopolitical position. The same issue applies to Joint Sword-2024B, where the strongly positive TSMC CAR contributes substantially to the event's positive average. A semiconductor-cycle explanation could account for why TSMC performs better than expected even during a geopolitical-risk event.

Semiconductor-cycle dynamics can explain asset-level divergence between TWSE and TSMC. The broader Taiwan market may respond to country-level risk, while TSMC may respond to expectations about advanced-node demand, customer relationships, or sector leadership. This helps explain why some events are negative for TWSE but less negative or positive for TSMC.

However, semiconductor-cycle dynamics do not fully explain the entire pattern. They are most relevant to TSMC, but the results also show patterns in TWSE. Some strategic-importance events are positive for both TWSE and TSMC, suggesting that the response may not be purely firm-specific. Semiconductor-cycle dynamics also do not explain why some risk events are strongly negative for the broader market. The rival explanation is therefore important but partial. It may account for some TSMC-specific positive observations, but it does not replace the need to interpret Taiwan-related political risk.

### 6.4.3 AI Boom and Technology Optimism

The AI boom and broader technology-sector optimism are closely related to semiconductor-cycle dynamics but deserve separate attention. The sample period overlaps with strong enthusiasm around artificial intelligence, advanced chips, data-centre investment, and high-performance computing. TSMC is central to this ecosystem. Positive returns around TSMC-linked events may therefore reflect global AI optimism rather than Taiwan-specific geopolitical interpretation.

This explanation is plausible because several positive observations are connected to TSMC, CHIPS Act support, and U.S. expansion. These events may have been interpreted not only as signs of Taiwan's strategic importance, but also as signals of growth in AI-related demand. If investors were especially optimistic about AI infrastructure and advanced semiconductors, TSMC could experience positive abnormal returns even during periods of geopolitical tension.

AI-sector optimism can also help explain why Joint Sword-2024B is positive, especially for TSMC. If TSMC was benefiting from broader technology optimism during the event window, then the positive TSMC response may not reflect adaptation to Taiwan-related military risk. It may reflect sector momentum that overwhelmed or offset the geopolitical signal.

However, AI optimism cannot fully explain all findings. It does not explain why risk events are negative on average, especially for TWSE. It also does not explain the negative response to BIS Advanced Semiconductor Export Controls, which is part of semiconductor geopolitics but appears among the most negative observations. If AI optimism alone were driving technology-related responses, one would expect technology-linked events to be more uniformly positive. The evidence instead suggests that technology optimism may be one influence among several, not a complete explanation.

### 6.4.4 Benchmark Specification

Benchmark specification is a methodological rival explanation. The dissertation calculates TWSE abnormal returns relative to NASDAQ and TSMC abnormal returns relative to SOX. These benchmarks are defensible as first-pass controls because NASDAQ captures broader technology-market movement and SOX captures semiconductor-sector movement. However, they are not the only possible benchmarks, and different choices could change the results.

This explanation is plausible because the raw and abnormal returns differ meaningfully. For strategic-importance events, raw average CAR is negative while abnormal CAR is positive. This means that the interpretation of results depends on benchmark adjustment. If an alternative benchmark were used, some abnormal returns might be smaller, larger, or different in sign. NASDAQ may be too U.S.-centric and technology-heavy to represent general global market conditions. SOX may be too closely tied to the same semiconductor forces that influence TSMC. Regional Asian benchmarks, global equity indices, MSCI Taiwan, or firm-level peer comparisons could produce different conclusions.

Benchmark specification can explain why some events look different after adjustment. It can also explain why TSMC appears to outperform or underperform depending on the comparator. This is especially relevant for strategic-importance events, where the shift from negative raw returns to positive abnormal returns is substantively important.

However, benchmark specification cannot fully explain the substantive pattern by itself. The fact that benchmark choice matters does not mean the observed abnormal returns are meaningless. It means they should be interpreted as conditional on the chosen model. Benchmark concerns also do not eliminate the substantive contrast between risk and strategic importance; rather, they require testing whether that contrast survives alternative specifications. Future robustness checks using alternative benchmarks would strengthen confidence in the findings, especially if the broad risk-versus-strategic-importance pattern remains visible across specifications. The current results should therefore be treated as informative but not definitive.

### 6.4.5 Event Selection Bias

Event-selection bias is another important concern. The event sample is manually constructed. This is appropriate for an exploratory dissertation, but it creates risks. Highly visible events may be overrepresented. Borderline events may be included or excluded based on researcher judgement. Crisis clusters may receive more attention than lower-salience events. Strategic-importance events are fewer than risk events, making category averages sensitive to a small number of observations.

This explanation is plausible because event selection shapes the empirical universe. The decision to include some events and exclude others affects the average returns by category. The handling of overlap also matters. The Pelosi crisis period includes warnings, the visit itself, trade restrictions, and military exercises. Treating these as separate events increases the number of observations but may also double-count a single crisis episode. Reducing overlap improves independence but reduces sample size.

Event-selection bias can explain why some patterns may appear stronger or weaker than they actually are. If the sample overrepresents severe crisis events, risk events may look more negative. If it includes only the most visible strategic-importance events, that category may appear more positive. If deviant cases are especially salient, they may receive disproportionate interpretive weight.

However, event-selection bias does not fully explain the findings. The event-selection criteria are substantively grounded in Taiwan Strait military activity, diplomatic shocks, political signalling, economic coercion, semiconductor geopolitics, and strategic technology competition. The deviant cases are not arbitrary; Joint Sword-2024B and BIS export controls are substantively relevant events. Event-selection concerns therefore require caution, transparency, and robustness checks, but they do not make the analysis invalid. They limit the strength of inference.

## 6.5 Theoretical Implications

The discussion has several implications for geopolitical-risk theory. The dissertation should not be interpreted as a rejection of geopolitical-risk theory. On the contrary, risk events are negative on average, and several of the most negative observations are associated with military escalation, coercion, or crisis dynamics. The results therefore support the basic claim that geopolitical risk matters. Rather, the dissertation introduces an additional interpretive layer between geopolitical shocks and market outcomes. The theoretical refinement is that geopolitical risk does not move directly or uniformly into market reaction.

The relationship suggested by the dissertation is:

Geopolitical Risk
↓
Interpretation
↓
Market Reaction

rather than:

Geopolitical Risk
↓
Market Reaction

This distinction matters because it places meaning between event and price. Investors do not simply observe objective risk and mechanically reprice assets. They may interpret whether an event is novel or familiar, threatening or contained, restrictive or supportive, surprising or anticipated. The same broad event category can therefore generate different market responses.

For sovereignty literature, the implication is that sovereignty ambiguity has economic consequences. Taiwan's contested status does not only shape diplomatic recognition or military risk. It also produces recurring market-relevant events. Grey-zone sovereignty generates repeated shocks because the underlying political status is unresolved. This connects sovereignty debates to observable financial-market behaviour without turning the dissertation into a legal-status argument.

For geoeconomics, the dissertation highlights the dual character of strategic importance. Taiwan's semiconductor position makes it economically valuable and politically exposed. Strategic industries may attract investment, policy support, and supply-chain protection, but they may also face restrictions, fragmentation, and regulatory uncertainty. The results are consistent with this duality: TSMC-related investment and CHIPS Act events are positive, while BIS export controls are negative.

For investor behaviour, the dissertation suggests that expectation formation and event meaning are central. Adaptation is one possible mechanism through which repeated shocks may change interpretation over time. If a class of events becomes familiar, investors may assign it less negative informational content unless it changes expected fundamentals. This is not the same as saying that investors ignore risk. It means that repeated exposure may alter how risk is processed.

The overall theoretical contribution can be expressed as:

Grey-Zone Sovereignty
↓
Recurring Shocks
↓
Adaptation
↓
Interpretation
↓
Market Reaction

This framework should be read as conditional and provisional. Grey-zone sovereignty explains why Taiwan-related events recur. Recurrence creates the possibility of adaptation. Adaptation may shape interpretation over time. Interpretation is the immediate mechanism through which events become market reactions. The framework does not eliminate threat or opportunity; it organises them within a broader account of how political ambiguity becomes priced.

The framework may also have relevance beyond Taiwan, although this dissertation does not test that broader claim. Taiwan is analytically useful because sovereignty ambiguity, recurring geopolitical pressure, and strategic economic importance are unusually visible in the same case. Similar dynamics may be relevant in other politically ambiguous or contested settings where repeated geopolitical shocks become familiar to investors over time. The point is not to generalise mechanically from Taiwan, but to suggest that recurring shocks and investor adaptation may not be unique to this case. Future comparative research would be needed to assess how far the framework travels.

## 6.6 Discussion Summary

This chapter has evaluated the main explanations for the empirical patterns reported in Chapter 5. The adaptation interpretation is plausible but not established. The sequence from Pelosi Visit to Joint Sword-2024B is compatible with the idea that repeated grey-zone shocks may become less novel and therefore less uniformly damaging, but it should be read as an illustrative pattern rather than a robust test. Joint Sword-2024B is especially important because it is a risk event that does not behave as a simple risk model would expect.

At the same time, rival explanations remain plausible. Anticipation effects may shift repricing before Day 0. Semiconductor-cycle dynamics may influence TSMC independently of Taiwan-related risk. AI-sector optimism may help explain positive technology-linked observations. Benchmark specification may affect abnormal-return estimates. Event-selection choices may shape the sample and the apparent strength of patterns. None of these explanations should be dismissed.

The strongest conclusion is therefore cautious. Objective geopolitical risk remains important, but investor interpretation appears central to how that risk is priced. Strategic importance also matters, but it is not uniformly positive. Adaptation may help explain why some repeated military demonstrations appear less damaging, but adaptation is inferred rather than directly observed.

Chapter 7 concludes the dissertation by returning to the research question and summarising the contribution. The conclusion does not claim that geopolitical risk no longer matters. Instead, it synthesises the dissertation's more conditional claim: under grey-zone sovereignty, the relationship between geopolitical risk and market outcomes depends on how repeated shocks are interpreted by investors.
