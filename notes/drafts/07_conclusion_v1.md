# Chapter 7: Conclusion

## 7.1 Revisiting the Research Question

This dissertation began with a puzzle: why do some Taiwan-related geopolitical shocks fail to generate persistent market damage despite elevated objective risk? The puzzle matters because Taiwan is widely understood as a major geopolitical flashpoint. Its contested status, exposure to Chinese military pressure, role in U.S.-China strategic competition, and central position in global semiconductor production all suggest that Taiwan-related shocks should be financially significant. A simple geopolitical-risk expectation would imply that events increasing cross-strait uncertainty should generally produce negative market responses.

The empirical findings complicate that expectation. Some events do behave as a conventional geopolitical-risk model would anticipate. The Pelosi crisis period, Joint Sword 2023, and other military or coercive events are associated with negative abnormal returns. Yet the results are not uniform. Joint Sword-2024B, despite being a military demonstration, is associated with positive abnormal returns in the main event window. Strategic-importance events are positive on average, but they too are not uniformly positive. The dissertation therefore does not ask whether geopolitical risk matters. It asks why the relationship between Taiwan-related geopolitical risk and market outcomes appears conditional rather than automatic.

The answer developed across the dissertation is that Taiwan's grey-zone sovereignty produces recurring shocks that investors must interpret. Grey-zone sovereignty refers to Taiwan's persistent condition of political ambiguity: Taiwan possesses substantial practical autonomy and strategic economic importance, but remains diplomatically constrained and politically contested. This condition generates recurring events that contain more than one possible market meaning. They may signal threat, but they may also signal strategic importance, policy support, repetition, anticipation, or constrained escalation. The dissertation has therefore examined whether observed market responses are consistent with an adaptation-based interpretation, while remaining cautious about causal claims.

## 7.2 Main Findings

The first main finding is that geopolitical-risk events are negative on average. In the event-study results, risk events show negative average abnormal returns for both TWSE and TSMC over the main [-7,+7] window. This finding is important because it confirms that geopolitical risk remains relevant. Taiwan-related events involving military demonstrations, diplomatic shocks, political signalling, or coercive economic pressure cannot be treated as market-neutral. The broader Taiwan market, represented by TWSE, appears especially sensitive to these events.

The second finding is that strategic-importance events are positive on average. Events connected to TSMC investment, CHIPS Act support, and semiconductor supply-chain restructuring are associated with positive average abnormal returns in the current sample. This pattern suggests that some Taiwan-related events may be interpreted through the lens of strategic value rather than only geopolitical vulnerability. Taiwan's position in semiconductor geopolitics can therefore generate positive market expectations when events are associated with policy support, investment, or supply-chain protection.

The third finding is that substantial variation exists within both categories. Risk events are not uniformly negative, and strategic-importance events are not uniformly positive. This variation is central to the dissertation's argument. It shows that classification alone does not determine market response. Risk and strategic importance are best understood as sources of investor interpretation rather than automatic predictors of market direction.

The fourth finding is that Joint Sword-2024B is the key deviant case. It is coded as a geopolitical-risk event because it was a military demonstration around Taiwan, yet it is associated with positive abnormal returns in the event window, especially for TSMC. This case is important because it directly challenges a simple risk-response model. It does not prove adaptation, but it provides a reason to ask whether repeated grey-zone military demonstrations may be interpreted differently over time.

The fifth finding is that later repeated shocks appear less uniformly negative than earlier shocks. The sequence from the Pelosi Visit to Joint Sword 2023, Joint Sword-2024A, and Joint Sword-2024B is compatible with an adaptation-based interpretation. The sequence should not be treated as a statistically robust trend, and it does not demonstrate investor learning. However, it is consistent with the possibility that repeated exposure reduces the novelty of some grey-zone shocks and alters the way investors interpret them.

## 7.3 Theoretical Contributions

The main theoretical contribution of the dissertation is the development of a grey-zone sovereignty framework linking sovereignty ambiguity to market behaviour. The framework can be summarised as follows:

Grey-Zone Sovereignty
↓
Recurring Shocks
↓
Adaptation
↓
Interpretation
↓
Market Reaction

This framework begins from the claim that Taiwan's ambiguous sovereignty status is not simply a legal or diplomatic condition. It is also a recurring source of politically significant events. Diplomatic visits, military exercises, export controls, investment announcements, and sovereignty-related political signals become market-relevant because they occur within an unresolved sovereignty environment. Grey-zone sovereignty therefore operates as the upstream condition that generates repeated market-relevant shocks.

The framework also clarifies that geopolitical risk remains important. The dissertation does not reject geopolitical-risk theory. Risk events are negative on average, and some of the most negative observations are associated with military escalation, coercive economic measures, or crisis dynamics. The contribution is more specific: risk does not translate into market outcomes in a direct or uniform way. It is mediated through investor interpretation.

Adaptation is treated as an inferred mechanism. The dissertation does not observe investor beliefs directly, and it does not claim to prove that investors learned to discount Taiwan-related risk. Instead, it argues that repeated shocks may become less novel, and that this reduced novelty may influence how investors interpret later events. Adaptation is therefore a plausible explanation for some observed patterns, but it remains subject to rival explanations.

The framework's value lies in its conditionality. It explains why Taiwan-related events can produce different financial responses even when they arise from the same structural condition. A military event may be interpreted as a threat, but a repeated military event may be interpreted as familiar signalling. A semiconductor event may be interpreted as an opportunity, but an export-control event may be interpreted as a constraint. The framework therefore links grey-zone sovereignty to market behaviour without reducing either sovereignty or markets to a single causal pathway.

## 7.4 Implications for International Relations and International Political Economy

For International Relations, the dissertation contributes to the study of sovereignty ambiguity, contested statehood, and grey-zone competition. Taiwan is a useful case because it demonstrates how contested sovereignty generates repeated political events without necessarily producing a formal crisis each time. Taiwan's status is persistently unresolved, and this unresolved status creates recurring opportunities for signalling, pressure, recognition disputes, and military demonstrations. The dissertation shows that these events do not remain confined to diplomacy or security studies; they also become visible in financial-market behaviour.

The IR implication is therefore that sovereignty ambiguity has market consequences. Contested statehood does not only matter because it affects legal status or military risk. It also shapes how political events are interpreted by economic actors. Grey-zone competition generates repeated signals, and those signals may become more or less market-damaging depending on how familiar, anticipated, or strategically embedded they appear.

For International Political Economy, the dissertation links geopolitical risk, financial markets, semiconductor politics, and economic security. Taiwan is useful because it is simultaneously vulnerable and strategically valuable. Its contested status creates risk, while its semiconductor centrality creates economic importance. This duality makes Taiwan a strong case for examining how markets respond when security risk and strategic economic value coexist.

The IPE implication is that markets should not be assumed to price geopolitical risk only as danger. In the Taiwan case, some events appear to highlight policy support, strategic industry value, and supply-chain protection. At the same time, strategic importance is not automatically positive. The BIS export-control case shows that semiconductor geopolitics can also be associated with restriction and uncertainty. The dissertation therefore suggests that market responses to geopolitical shocks should be analysed as interpretive and conditional, especially in sectors tied to economic security.

## 7.5 Limitations

The dissertation has several limitations. The first is small sample size. The abnormal-return analysis contains a limited number of events, and the strategic-importance category is especially small. This means that averages are sensitive to individual observations and should not be treated as stable estimates.

The second limitation concerns event classification. The distinction between geopolitical-risk events and strategic-importance events is theoretically useful, but it simplifies complex events. Some events contain both risk and strategic-importance elements. Classification choices therefore shape the empirical patterns and should be treated as transparent but contestable.

The third limitation is that adaptation is inferred rather than observed. The dissertation does not include investor surveys, analyst-expectation data, or direct evidence of investor beliefs. It observes market reactions and evaluates whether they are compatible with adaptation. This is a necessary but limited form of inference.

The fourth limitation is benchmark dependence. The abnormal returns depend on the selected benchmarks, with TWSE adjusted against NASDAQ and TSMC adjusted against SOX. These are defensible choices, but alternative benchmarks could change the magnitude or even the direction of some abnormal returns.

The fifth limitation is anticipation. Some Taiwan-related events may have been expected before the event date. If investors priced an event before Day 0, the event-window response may understate or misrepresent the market reaction. This is especially relevant for repeated military exercises and politically anticipated responses.

The sixth limitation concerns broader market conditions. The sample period overlaps with AI-sector optimism and semiconductor-cycle dynamics. Positive TSMC-related returns may partly reflect global technology-market conditions rather than Taiwan-specific geopolitical interpretation. These limitations are not fatal flaws, but they mean that the findings should be interpreted cautiously.

## 7.6 Future Research

Future research should first expand the event sample. A larger dataset would allow stronger comparison across event categories, more systematic analysis of repeated shocks, and more stable estimates of average effects. It would also make it possible to separate military demonstrations, diplomatic shocks, economic coercion, and technology-policy events more precisely.

Second, future work should pursue cross-national comparison. Taiwan is a critical case, but the framework could be examined in other contexts where contested sovereignty, geopolitical pressure, and economic importance intersect. Cases involving other politically ambiguous or strategically contested territories may help assess whether grey-zone sovereignty produces similar market dynamics elsewhere.

Third, future research should incorporate more direct evidence of investor expectations. Investor surveys, analyst reports, media-event coding, or measures of news anticipation could help distinguish adaptation from anticipation. This would strengthen the empirical basis for claims about investor interpretation.

Fourth, future work should test alternative benchmarks and abnormal-return models. Comparing results across regional indices, global technology benchmarks, semiconductor peers, and market-model specifications would help assess whether the current findings are robust to benchmark choice.

Finally, future research could extend the asset coverage beyond equities. Exchange rates, credit default swaps, options-implied volatility, and sector-level indices may reveal different dimensions of Taiwan-related risk. Such extensions would help determine whether adaptation-like patterns appear across markets or are specific to equity responses.

## 7.7 Final Conclusion

This dissertation does not argue that geopolitical risk no longer matters. On the contrary, risk events are negative on average, and several Taiwan-related shocks are associated with substantial market weakness. The central claim is more cautious: under conditions of grey-zone sovereignty, the relationship between geopolitical risk and market outcomes is conditional rather than automatic.

Taiwan's ambiguous sovereignty status generates recurring shocks. These shocks are interpreted by investors in relation to threat, strategic importance, repetition, and surprise. Repeated shocks may become less damaging when investors interpret them as familiar, anticipated, or strategically embedded events. Yet this remains an interpretation of the observed evidence, not a proven causal mechanism.

The dissertation's contribution is therefore to show that Taiwan-related market reactions cannot be understood through geopolitical risk alone. Grey-zone sovereignty creates vulnerability, but it also creates strategic importance. Markets appear to respond to both. The task for future research is to test more rigorously how these competing interpretations shape market behaviour. For now, the evidence supports a cautious conclusion: Taiwan-related geopolitical shocks matter for financial markets, but their effects depend on how those shocks are interpreted within a recurring grey-zone sovereignty environment.
