# Chapter 3: Theoretical Framework

## 3.1 Grey-Zone Sovereignty as the Independent Variable

This chapter develops the theoretical framework that guides the dissertation. The central claim is that Taiwan's grey-zone sovereignty operates as the independent variable because it is the underlying political condition from which the observed geopolitical events emerge. Grey-zone sovereignty is defined here as a persistent condition of political ambiguity that simultaneously generates recurring geopolitical risk and strategic economic importance. The concept is not used simply as a descriptive label for Taiwan's unusual international status, nor as a legal argument about whether Taiwan should be classified as a state. It is used as an explanatory framework for understanding why Taiwan-related events repeatedly become financially salient.

Taiwan possesses many attributes normally associated with sovereign statehood: democratic institutions, independent policy-making capacity, territorial administration, a military, and a highly developed economy. Yet Taiwan lacks broad formal diplomatic recognition and remains subject to the People's Republic of China's sovereignty claim. The United States supports Taiwan's security without resolving the question of formal statehood. This produces a political condition that is neither ordinary sovereignty nor simple dependence. Taiwan exercises substantial autonomy, but that autonomy is persistently contested.

Grey-zone sovereignty should therefore be treated as the independent variable rather than as background context. It precedes the specific events analysed in the event study and explains why those events occur in the first place. A Pelosi visit, a Joint Sword exercise, an inauguration speech, an export-control announcement, or a TSMC investment decision becomes market-relevant because it takes place within a persistent structure of unresolved sovereignty, U.S.-China competition, and Taiwan's strategic economic centrality. Grey-zone sovereignty is the upstream condition that makes a class of recurring Taiwan-related shocks possible.

The dependent variable is financial-market reaction, measured through abnormal returns for the TWSE and TSMC around Taiwan-related event windows. The intervening process is investor interpretation. Grey-zone sovereignty does not directly determine returns in a mechanical way. Rather, it generates events that investors interpret as threat, strategic importance, or familiar repetition. The framework therefore moves from political condition to recurring event, from recurring event to investor interpretation, and from investor interpretation to market reaction.

This ordering matters because it prevents the analysis from reducing Taiwan to a simple risk variable. The findings already suggest that such a model is insufficient. Risk events are negative on average, but not uniformly negative. Strategic-importance events are positive on average, but not uniformly positive. Grey-zone sovereignty is therefore best understood as a generator of ambiguous market signals whose effects depend on interpretation.

## 3.2 From Grey-Zone Sovereignty to Recurring Shocks

Grey-zone sovereignty generates recurring shocks because Taiwan's political ambiguity is not episodic. It is a durable condition of international politics. Taiwan's unresolved status creates incentives for repeated signalling by multiple actors. The People's Republic of China has incentives to demonstrate that Taiwan remains within its claimed sovereignty domain and to deter moves that could be interpreted as formal independence. Taiwan's government has incentives to maintain autonomy, deepen international partnerships, and signal democratic legitimacy. The United States has incentives to support Taiwan while avoiding an explicit transformation of Taiwan's legal status. Other states and firms have incentives to engage Taiwan economically while managing diplomatic and strategic risk. These overlapping incentives produce recurring events rather than isolated crises.

The recurrence of Taiwan-related shocks is built into the structure of the issue. Military demonstrations, diplomatic protests, sanctions, export controls, political speeches, and semiconductor-policy announcements are not random disturbances. They are recurring expressions of the unresolved sovereignty environment. Each event may have a discrete date suitable for event-study analysis, but the class of events is generated by a continuing political condition. In a one-off crisis, markets might react primarily to severity. In a recurring grey-zone environment, markets may also react to whether an event resembles earlier episodes and whether it changes expectations.

This helps connect geopolitical risk with strategic importance. Taiwan's grey-zone sovereignty creates risk because its status remains contested and cross-strait escalation would have severe consequences. At the same time, the same ambiguity reinforces Taiwan's strategic importance. Taiwan is not merely exposed to U.S.-China competition; it is one of the key sites through which that competition is organised. Its semiconductor sector, especially TSMC, gives Taiwan global economic significance. Events connected to Taiwan may therefore signal not only danger but also protection, policy support, or technological centrality.

This dual character is central to the framework. A standard geopolitical-risk account would classify Taiwan-related military and diplomatic events as risk shocks and expect negative market reactions. This dissertation accepts that expectation as a baseline but argues that it is incomplete. Some Taiwan-related events heighten the perceived probability of coercion or conflict. Others demonstrate that Taiwan remains strategically indispensable. Some do both.

Recurring shocks also create the conditions for adaptation. If markets observe repeated crisis patterns, military exercises, diplomatic protests, and semiconductor-policy interventions, investors may begin to distinguish between shocks that materially alter fundamentals and shocks that belong to a familiar repertoire of grey-zone signalling. This does not mean that objective risk disappears. It means that market interpretation may change as similar events recur.

## 3.3 Adaptation and the Changing Interpretation of Risk

Adaptation is the primary mechanism in the theoretical framework because it addresses the dissertation's central puzzle most directly. The puzzle is not whether Taiwan-related risk events ever generate negative returns. They do. The puzzle is why some high-risk events fail to generate persistent market damage. Adaptation offers a parsimonious explanation: repeated grey-zone shocks become less novel, and less novel shocks are less likely to be interpreted as fundamental changes in the risk environment.

In this framework, adaptation refers to a change in investor interpretation produced by repeated exposure to similar events. Early or unusual events are more likely to be interpreted as threats because investors have less precedent for evaluating their implications. The Pelosi Visit and the immediate PLA response in 2022 can be understood in this way. Later exercises, such as Joint Sword 2023, Joint Sword-2024A, and Joint Sword-2024B, occurred within a more established pattern of Chinese military signalling around Taiwan-related political events. As this pattern became more familiar, investors may have become more capable of distinguishing between symbolic coercive signalling and a material change in the probability of conflict.

Adaptation does not imply complacency. It does not mean investors believe that cross-strait risk is unimportant. Rather, it means that repeated exposure can reduce the informational shock value of an event. A military exercise may still be objectively serious, but if it resembles previous exercises and does not appear to alter expected fundamentals, markets may react less negatively than a simple risk model would predict. This distinction separates objective risk from perceived market novelty. The same event can remain strategically serious while becoming less surprising or less informative to investors.

Adaptation also differs from opportunity and anticipation. Opportunity refers to positive interpretation based on strategic economic value, policy support, or supply-chain importance. Adaptation refers to the weakening of negative interpretation as events become familiar. Anticipation means that an event was expected before the event window and may already have been priced into markets. These mechanisms can produce similar empirical patterns. If Joint Sword-2024B generated a positive or muted abnormal return, this may indicate adaptation, but it may also indicate prior pricing, favourable semiconductor conditions, or benchmark limitations.

## 3.4 Investor Interpretation and Market Reaction

The theoretical framework places investor interpretation between geopolitical events and financial-market reaction. This is the central departure from a mechanical risk model. Markets do not respond to geopolitical events as objective facts alone. They respond to the expected implications of those events for future cash flows, discount rates, policy support, supply-chain stability, sector performance, and conflict probability. A market reaction is therefore an interpreted response to information.

Investor interpretation can take at least four forms in this dissertation. The first is threat: the perception that an event raises the probability of conflict, coercion, disruption, or policy instability. Threat should generally be associated with negative abnormal returns, particularly for Taiwan-wide market indicators such as the TWSE. The second is opportunity: the perception that an event signals Taiwan's strategic value, policy support for semiconductor capacity, supply-chain resilience, or long-term demand for technology firms. Opportunity should generally be associated with positive abnormal returns, particularly for TSMC.

The third form is adaptation: the interpretation of an event as part of an established grey-zone repertoire rather than as a new escalation. Adaptation should reduce the negative effect of risk events over time, especially within repeated event families such as PLA military demonstrations after sovereignty-related political triggers. The fourth form is surprise: whether an event changes expectations. A severe event that was fully anticipated may produce little immediate abnormal return, while a less severe but unexpected event may produce a larger reaction. Surprise therefore affects the magnitude of market movement, while threat and opportunity more directly shape its direction.

This interpretive framework explains why the same event category may generate different outcomes. Military demonstrations are expected to be negative under a threat model, but they may become less negative if they are repeated, anticipated, or interpreted as contained signalling. Semiconductor-policy events are expected to be positive under an opportunity model, but they may be negative if investors interpret them as restrictive, costly, or evidence of technological fragmentation. The BIS Advanced Semiconductor Export Controls case is important because it belongs to semiconductor geopolitics, yet its market meaning may be closer to constraint than opportunity.

Interpretation also explains why TWSE and TSMC may respond differently. The TWSE captures broader Taiwan market exposure and may be more sensitive to general country risk. TSMC captures firm-level and sector-specific expectations about semiconductor demand, supply-chain restructuring, industrial policy, and AI-related growth. A Taiwan-related event can therefore be negative for the broader market while less negative or even positive for TSMC, or vice versa. This asset-specific variation supports the argument that markets are not pricing a single undifferentiated Taiwan risk factor.

## 3.5 Adaptation as an Inferred Mechanism

Adaptation is not directly observed in the data. The dissertation observes event classifications, abnormal returns, cumulative abnormal returns, event repetition, and preliminary surprise coding. It does not observe investor beliefs directly. This is why adaptation must be treated as an inferred mechanism rather than a demonstrated psychological process. The empirical evidence can show that later repeated shocks generate weaker negative abnormal returns than earlier shocks. It can show that a high-risk event such as Joint Sword-2024B does not behave as a simple risk model would expect. It can show that patterns are consistent with changing interpretation. It cannot, by itself, prove that investors consciously adapted.

This distinction is essential for the credibility of the argument. A dissertation-level theory should not treat market outcomes as transparent evidence of investor psychology. Abnormal returns are observable; interpretation is inferred. The inference is strengthened when events belong to a recognisable repeated family, later events are less negative than earlier comparable events, the pattern survives benchmark adjustment, rival explanations are considered, and surprise coding helps distinguish adaptation from prior pricing.

The current framework therefore presents adaptation as a plausible and testable interpretation of observed market patterns. It does not treat adaptation as proven. The language of the theory is deliberately conditional: repeated grey-zone shocks may become less damaging because investors may learn to interpret them as familiar and contained. The theory organises the evidence and generates hypotheses for further testing, but it remains open to rival explanations.

## 3.6 Difference from Standard Geopolitical-Risk Theory

The framework differs from standard geopolitical-risk theory in three ways. First, it treats geopolitical risk as mediated rather than direct. Standard geopolitical-risk theory often implies that greater risk should generate more negative market outcomes because uncertainty raises discount rates, lowers expected investment, and increases the probability of disruption. This dissertation accepts that logic as a baseline but argues that it does not travel cleanly to a recurring grey-zone case such as Taiwan. Risk must be interpreted before it becomes market reaction.

Second, the framework treats Taiwan's political ambiguity as dual rather than purely negative. Grey-zone sovereignty generates vulnerability, but it also generates strategic importance. Taiwan is exposed because its status is contested; it is valuable because its semiconductor centrality makes it indispensable to U.S.-China competition and global technology supply chains. Standard risk theory captures the vulnerability side more easily than the strategic-importance side.

Third, the framework introduces repetition and adaptation. Standard geopolitical-risk theory is strongest when analysing discrete shocks that introduce new uncertainty. Taiwan-related events, however, often repeat. Repetition changes the informational content of a shock. A repeated exercise or diplomatic protest may be risky in an objective sense but less informative than an unprecedented escalation. This is why the dissertation gives adaptation a central role. It explains why the relationship between objective risk and market damage may weaken over time.

The resulting theory can be summarised as follows. Taiwan's grey-zone sovereignty produces recurring geopolitical and geoeconomic shocks. These shocks contain ambiguous market signals. Investors interpret them through threat, opportunity, adaptation, and surprise. Market reactions depend on this interpretation rather than on objective event type alone. The framework therefore predicts heterogeneity: risk events should be negative on average, but not uniformly negative; strategic-importance events should be positive on average, but not uniformly positive; repeated shocks should become less damaging when they resemble prior grey-zone patterns and do not change expected fundamentals.

## 3.7 Hypotheses

The framework generates four hypotheses for empirical evaluation. These hypotheses are not claims that the dissertation has already proven. They state the expectations implied by the theory and provide a structure for interpreting the event-study results.

H1, the threat hypothesis, is that Taiwan-related geopolitical-risk events are associated with negative abnormal returns. Events involving military demonstrations, diplomatic shocks, economic coercion, or sovereignty-related political signalling should, on average, produce more negative TWSE and TSMC abnormal returns than events not interpreted as threats.

H2, the strategic-importance hypothesis, is that Taiwan-related strategic-importance events are associated with more positive abnormal returns. Events involving semiconductor geopolitics, CHIPS Act support, TSMC strategic investment, supply-chain restructuring, or technology competition should, on average, produce more positive abnormal returns, especially for TSMC.

H3, the adaptation hypothesis, is that repeated Taiwan-related grey-zone shocks are associated with weaker negative abnormal returns over time. As event repetition increases within comparable event families, especially military demonstrations and sovereignty-related signalling, cumulative abnormal returns should become less negative than a simple geopolitical-risk model would predict.

H4, the surprise hypothesis, is that more surprising Taiwan-related events are associated with larger abnormal-return movements. Surprise should affect the magnitude of reaction, whether negative or positive, because events that change expectations should contain more new information than events that were fully anticipated.

Together, these hypotheses define the dissertation's theoretical claim. Grey-zone sovereignty is the structural independent variable because it generates the recurring ambiguous events that markets must price. Adaptation is the primary mechanism because it explains why repeated high-risk events may become less damaging. Investor interpretation is the intervening process because it determines whether a given event is priced as threat, opportunity, familiar repetition, or surprise.
