# Chapter 2: Literature Review

## 2.1 Sovereignty and Taiwan

The starting point for this dissertation is the problem of sovereignty. In classical political theory, sovereignty is associated with final authority over a political community. Hobbes located sovereignty in the capacity to establish order against insecurity, treating political authority as the necessary solution to the problem of disorder. In this tradition, sovereignty is tied to the ability to command, protect, and organise collective life. Weber's account shifts the emphasis towards the modern state as an institutional form, defined by the claim to the legitimate monopoly of physical force within a given territory. Taken together, Hobbes and Weber provide the conventional vocabulary through which sovereignty is often understood: authority, territory, coercive capacity, and political order.

Taiwan complicates this vocabulary. It possesses many of the practical attributes associated with sovereign authority. It has a democratically elected government, a professional military, independent economic policy, territorial administration, and functioning legal and bureaucratic institutions. In Weberian terms, it exercises many of the domestic functions of statehood. Yet the international status of Taiwan remains unresolved. It lacks broad diplomatic recognition, is claimed by the People's Republic of China, and is embedded within a strategic relationship in which the United States supports Taiwan without fully resolving the question of formal sovereignty. Taiwan therefore demonstrates that sovereignty cannot be understood only as domestic capacity. It is also a relational and international condition.

Wendt's constructivist approach is useful here because it treats sovereignty as a social institution rather than merely a material fact. Sovereignty depends not only on control, but also on recognition by others. States exist in a social world where status, legitimacy, and identity are produced through interaction. This insight is central to the Taiwan case. Taiwan's internal political authority is substantial, but its external recognition is limited. It exists as a polity whose practical autonomy is difficult to deny, but whose formal status is diplomatically constrained. Taiwan's position therefore highlights the gap between empirical sovereignty and recognised sovereignty.

Contemporary sovereignty scholarship sharpens this point. Krasner's account of sovereignty as organised hypocrisy is especially useful because it challenges the idea that formal sovereignty and political practice necessarily align. Sovereignty is often invoked as a stable legal principle, yet in practice it is repeatedly compromised, negotiated, violated, or selectively recognised. The importance of Krasner's argument for this dissertation is not that Taiwan should be treated as a legal exception, but that Taiwan exposes the divergence between formal sovereignty and political reality. Taiwan exercises many capacities associated with sovereign government, but its formal recognition remains constrained by diplomatic practice and great-power politics. This divergence generates recurring politically significant events because actors continually signal, contest, avoid, or reinterpret Taiwan's status. Diplomatic visits, military exercises, recognition disputes, and policy statements become consequential precisely because the formal category of sovereignty does not resolve the political reality.

Taiwan sovereignty literature, including the work associated with Shen and Chiang, develops this problem more directly. Shen's contribution lies in connecting Taiwan's statehood question to the criteria and politics of international recognition. Taiwan cannot be understood simply through a checklist of state attributes, because its status is shaped by the political refusal of many states to recognise it formally. Chiang's more contemporary work extends this discussion into the present context of cross-strait relations, democratic identity, and Taiwan's changing international position. These scholars help explain why Taiwan occupies a distinctive position: it is not simply a normal state, but it is also not reducible to an object of Chinese policy. It is an autonomous political community whose sovereignty is practised domestically but contested internationally.

The limitation of this sovereignty literature is that it is primarily concerned with status, recognition, statehood, and identity. These are necessary foundations, but they do not directly explain financial-market behaviour. The literature explains why Taiwan's political position is ambiguous and why events involving Taiwan carry unusual symbolic and strategic weight. It does not explain why one sovereignty-related shock produces negative abnormal returns while another does not. Nor does it explain why investors may sometimes interpret Taiwan's contested position as a source of opportunity rather than only as a source of danger.

This dissertation therefore uses sovereignty theory not to resolve the legal status of Taiwan, but to develop the concept of grey-zone sovereignty. Grey-zone sovereignty refers to a persistent condition of political ambiguity that simultaneously generates recurring geopolitical risk and strategic economic importance. The concept moves beyond the question of whether Taiwan is or is not a state. Instead, it asks how Taiwan's ambiguous status generates repeated political events that markets must interpret. A diplomatic visit, a military exercise, an inauguration speech, or a recognition dispute becomes market-relevant because it occurs within an unresolved sovereignty environment. At the same time, Taiwan's status is tied to its economic centrality, especially in semiconductors. Grey-zone sovereignty therefore provides the political foundation for the dissertation's central puzzle: why do some Taiwan-related geopolitical shocks fail to generate persistent market damage despite elevated objective risk?

## 2.2 Geopolitical Risk and Financial Markets

The second body of literature concerns geopolitical risk and financial markets. Geopolitical risk research begins from the proposition that political conflict, military tension, terrorism, war, sanctions, and interstate rivalry can affect economic expectations and asset prices. Caldara and Iacoviello's work is central because it provides a systematic way to conceptualise and measure geopolitical risk. Their framework treats geopolitical risk as a form of uncertainty that can influence investment, consumption, volatility, and financial-market behaviour. The central intuition is that investors may discount assets more heavily when political shocks raise uncertainty over future cash flows, supply chains, trade conditions, or macroeconomic stability.

Event-study literature extends this logic into an empirical strategy. If markets process new information quickly, returns around an event window can be used to assess how investors react to specific shocks. Political events such as elections, sanctions, terrorist attacks, military conflicts, or diplomatic crises can be examined by comparing actual returns with expected or benchmark-adjusted returns. This approach is particularly useful for the present dissertation because Taiwan-related geopolitical events have identifiable dates. The Pelosi Visit, Joint Sword exercises, export-control announcements, and TSMC policy events can be located in time, allowing the analysis to examine abnormal returns around those dates.

The geopolitical-risk literature gives strong reasons to expect Taiwan-related shocks to place downward pressure on markets. Taiwan is a site of persistent military pressure and diplomatic tension. Cross-strait escalation could disrupt trade, damage investor confidence, threaten supply chains, and raise the probability of conflict. It is therefore reasonable to expect higher geopolitical risk to be associated, on average, with more negative market reactions. This expectation is intuitive and is partly supported by the empirical findings. The Pelosi Visit, the China trade restrictions after the visit, the PLA exercises after the visit, Joint Sword 2023, and Strait Thunder-2025A all show patterns broadly consistent with the threat channel. Risk events are negative on average in the current event-study results.

However, this expectation should not be overstated. The geopolitical-risk literature does not require perfectly uniform negative outcomes in every event window, and sophisticated event-study research recognises that market responses depend on expectations, timing, benchmarks, co-occurring news, and the informational content of the event. The problem revealed by the Taiwan case is therefore more precise. The findings do not simply show occasional noise around an otherwise stable risk relationship. They show theoretically meaningful variation across events that are all connected to Taiwan's contested status. Some risk events are positive or mixed. Joint Sword-2024B is the most important example because it is a military demonstration but produces positive abnormal returns over the current event window. Lai's U.S. transit also fails to generate the negative pattern expected from a diplomatic-risk event. At the same time, BIS Advanced Semiconductor Export Controls, which is classified as a strategic-importance event, produces negative abnormal returns. These cases challenge a simple transmission model from geopolitical risk to market damage.

The limitation of the geopolitical-risk literature is therefore not that it is wrong, nor that it crudely assumes automatic market decline. Rather, the limitation is that it often leaves underdeveloped the interpretive process through which political risk becomes priced. Higher risk tends to increase uncertainty, and uncertainty often has negative financial effects, but the meaning of a specific event still has to be assessed by investors. A military exercise may be interpreted as a genuine escalation, but it may also be interpreted as a familiar form of grey-zone signalling. A technology-policy event may signal support for a strategic industry, but it may also signal restriction, fragmentation, or compliance costs. Event-study methods can detect market reactions, but they do not by themselves explain why similar shocks produce different interpretations.

This dissertation builds on geopolitical-risk literature by retaining its core insight that political shocks matter for markets, while revising the expectation that risk is sufficient to predict the direction of market reaction. The Taiwan case suggests that risk is not an automatic determinant of market outcome. It is a source of investor interpretation. This distinction is central to the grey-zone sovereignty framework. Taiwan-related events are risky because they emerge from unresolved sovereignty and strategic rivalry, but their market effects depend on whether investors interpret them as novel threats, repeated signals, opportunities, anticipated developments, or evidence of changing strategic value.

## 2.3 Geoeconomics and Semiconductor Politics

The third literature concerns geoeconomics, economic statecraft, and semiconductor politics. Baldwin's work on economic statecraft is important because it treats economic instruments as tools of political power. States use trade, finance, sanctions, investment restrictions, market access, and technology controls to influence other actors. In this view, economic policy is not separate from security. It is one of the means through which strategic competition is conducted. This insight is directly relevant to Taiwan, where economic interdependence, semiconductor production, export controls, and investment policy are inseparable from cross-strait and U.S.-China politics.

Wigell's geoeconomic framework similarly emphasises the fusion of economic and security logics. Geoeconomics refers to the use of economic instruments for strategic purposes and to the transformation of markets into arenas of power politics. The Taiwan case sits squarely within this literature. China's trade restrictions after the Pelosi Visit, U.S. semiconductor export controls, CHIPS Act subsidies, and TSMC's overseas expansion all show that economic events are not merely commercial. They are embedded in wider strategic competition. Taiwan's markets are therefore exposed not only to military risk, but also to financial statecraft, technology policy, and supply-chain politics.

Solís's work on economic security and regional order is relevant because it highlights the increasing importance of supply chains, technology governance, and economic resilience in contemporary international politics. In an era of strategic competition, states seek to secure critical technologies and reduce dependence on adversaries or vulnerable chokepoints. Taiwan is central to this shift because its semiconductor industry is both economically indispensable and geopolitically exposed. The location of advanced chip production in Taiwan creates a strategic dilemma: Taiwan's centrality makes it valuable, but that same centrality makes cross-strait risk globally consequential.

This economic-security literature is important because it explains why strategic importance can generate positive market expectations. Strategic industries are not valued only because they are profitable; they may also attract state support, subsidies, investment incentives, export-policy protection, infrastructure spending, and diplomatic attention. In the semiconductor sector, geopolitical rivalry can increase the perceived importance of firms and supply chains that governments regard as critical. Investors may therefore interpret some Taiwan-related technology events as signals of industrial policy support, supply-chain protection, or long-run demand. Strategic importance can thus become a market-positive signal when it implies that states will mobilise resources to preserve, expand, or relocate critical capacity.

Bellocchi's work on the strategic importance of Taiwan helps connect this geoeconomic literature to the Taiwan-specific case. Taiwan's value does not derive only from its democratic identity or its position in U.S.-China rivalry. It also derives from its role in the technological infrastructure of the global economy. TSMC's position in advanced semiconductor manufacturing means that Taiwan is not just a possible site of conflict. It is also a critical node in the production of AI hardware, high-performance computing, and advanced industrial systems. Semiconductor politics therefore becomes a key part of the dissertation's explanation.

This literature explains why strategic-importance events can be associated with positive abnormal returns. TSMC Arizona expansion, TSMC CHIPS Act preliminary terms, and TSMC U.S. expansion are not ordinary corporate announcements. They signal industrial policy, supply-chain restructuring, and the strategic value of Taiwan-linked semiconductor capacity. Investors may interpret these events as evidence of policy support, long-run demand, subsidies, supply-chain protection, or the protection of strategic assets. This helps explain why strategic-importance events are positive on average in the current results.

Yet this literature also has limitations. It can overstate the positive implications of strategic importance if it assumes that being strategically valuable is always beneficial for markets. The BIS Advanced Semiconductor Export Controls case shows why that assumption is too simple. Export controls are clearly part of semiconductor geopolitics, but they can signal restriction and uncertainty as much as opportunity. Strategic importance may attract investment and policy support, but it may also produce regulatory burdens, supply-chain fragmentation, compliance costs, and exposure to great-power rivalry. In other words, semiconductor geopolitics does not automatically generate positive market reactions.

For the grey-zone sovereignty framework, the geoeconomic and semiconductor literatures provide the second force operating alongside risk. Taiwan's political ambiguity creates vulnerability, but its economic centrality creates strategic value. The dissertation's contribution is to show that both forces are present, and that neither determines market outcomes automatically. Strategic importance is a source of interpretation. Whether it becomes positive or negative depends on how investors read the event: as support, subsidy, constraint, opportunity, fragmentation, or evidence of future policy risk.

## 2.4 Investor Interpretation and Adaptation

The most important literature for the dissertation's mechanism concerns investor interpretation and adaptation. Whereas geopolitical-risk literature explains why risk should matter, and semiconductor politics explains why Taiwan can be strategically valuable, interpretation and adaptation explain why similar events can produce different reactions. Financial markets do not respond to objective events in a vacuum. They respond to events relative to expectations, prior information, recent precedent, and broader market narratives.

Taleb's work on uncertainty, rare events, and the limits of prediction is useful because it highlights the importance of surprise and non-linear reaction. Markets may be more vulnerable to events that fall outside expected patterns than to events that are severe but anticipated. The relevance to Taiwan is clear. A military exercise that occurs after repeated warnings and resembles previous exercises may be less informative than a sudden escalation that changes expectations. Severity and surprise are therefore analytically distinct. A high-risk event may generate a limited reaction if investors expected it, while a less severe event may produce a sharper response if it changes the perceived distribution of future outcomes.

Behavioural finance literature reinforces this point by showing that investors do not process information mechanically. Market reactions are shaped by attention, salience, prior beliefs, framing, representativeness, and learning. Investors may overreact to novel shocks and underreact to familiar ones. They may also use recent precedent to classify events. If earlier Taiwan Strait crises produced temporary disruption but not lasting market damage, later episodes may be interpreted as manageable unless they contain new information. This logic is central to the distinction between adaptation and anticipation. Anticipation implies that the event was expected and therefore priced before Day 0. Adaptation implies that repeated exposure changes the meaning investors assign to a class of events.

It is important, however, to distinguish adaptation from interpretation. Interpretation is the immediate mechanism that produces market reaction. Investors interpret an event as a threat, an opportunity, a familiar signal, a policy constraint, or a surprise, and those interpretations are reflected in prices. Adaptation is a deeper mechanism that changes interpretation over time. It occurs through repeated exposure, learning, expectation formation, and reduced novelty. A later military exercise may still be interpreted as risky, but if investors have seen similar exercises before and regard them as part of a recurring grey-zone repertoire, the threat interpretation may be weaker. Adaptation therefore influences interpretation; it is not a competing explanation to interpretation.

This clarification matters for the dissertation's theoretical argument. The sequence from the Pelosi Visit to Joint Sword 2023, Joint Sword-2024A, and Joint Sword-2024B suggests that repeated military demonstrations may become less damaging over time. The evidence is limited and should not be overstated, but the pattern motivates the adaptation hypothesis. Early shocks may be interpreted as more threatening because they are novel or less familiar. Later shocks may be interpreted as part of a repeated repertoire of grey-zone pressure. If investors learn that such events do not necessarily alter fundamentals, the interpretation of the event changes, and the market reaction may weaken.

At the same time, the adaptation literature has its own limitations. It is difficult to separate adaptation from anticipation. Later Joint Sword exercises may have produced weaker reactions not because investors learned to treat them as manageable, but because they were expected before they occurred. If a PRC military response after a Taiwan political event is widely anticipated, then the event window may miss the relevant repricing. This is why the dissertation develops a surprise-coding protocol. Surprise must be measured explicitly if adaptation is to be distinguished from the simpler claim that markets had already priced the event.

Investor interpretation also helps explain why TWSE and TSMC sometimes diverge. The broad Taiwan equity market may be more sensitive to general geopolitical risk, while TSMC may be interpreted through semiconductor demand, AI expectations, strategic investment, and supply-chain policy. A single Taiwan-related event can therefore produce different asset-level reactions. This divergence is not a methodological inconvenience; it is theoretically important. It indicates that markets are not pricing a single undifferentiated Taiwan risk factor. They are interpreting different assets through different mixtures of threat, opportunity, adaptation, and surprise.

For the grey-zone sovereignty framework, the interpretation and adaptation literature provides the central mechanism. Grey-zone sovereignty produces recurring ambiguous events. Geopolitical risk and strategic importance provide the competing sources of meaning. Adaptation changes the way repeated events are understood over time, while interpretation is the immediate process through which those meanings are priced. Threat remains the baseline interpretation, opportunity explains some strategic-importance events, and surprise conditions the size of reactions. Adaptation, however, is the mechanism that most directly addresses the central puzzle because it explains why repeated high-risk events may become less damaging without assuming that objective risk has disappeared.

## 2.5 Literature Gap

The gap addressed by this dissertation is not that Taiwan has been ignored. Taiwan has been widely studied in relation to sovereignty, recognition, cross-strait relations, democratisation, deterrence, and U.S.-China rivalry. Nor is the gap simply that financial markets have ignored geopolitics. Geopolitical-risk research and event-study methods already examine how political shocks affect asset prices. The gap lies in the assumed relationship between geopolitical risk and market outcomes.

Much of the existing literature expects geopolitical risk to exert negative pressure on financial markets because risk increases uncertainty, raises the perceived probability of disruption, and can reduce investor confidence. This is a reasonable starting point, and the present findings partly support it. Yet the Taiwan case reveals its limits. Risk events are negative on average, but not uniformly negative. Strategic-importance events are positive on average, but not uniformly positive. Some military events produce positive abnormal returns, while some technology events produce negative abnormal returns. These findings suggest that the relationship between political shock and market reaction is conditioned by interpretation.

Each literature reviewed above contributes to the dissertation, but each leaves part of the puzzle unresolved. Sovereignty literature explains why Taiwan's status is ambiguous, but not how investors price that ambiguity. Geopolitical-risk literature explains why risk should matter, but not why high-risk events sometimes fail to produce persistent damage. Geoeconomics explains why economic instruments and markets are part of strategic competition, but not why the direction of event-level market response varies. Semiconductor politics explains Taiwan's strategic value, but not why strategic-importance events can be negative. Behavioural finance and adaptation literature explains expectation-based variation, but not why Taiwan produces recurring ambiguous shocks in the first place.

This dissertation contributes a grey-zone sovereignty framework linking sovereignty ambiguity, recurring geopolitical shocks, investor adaptation, investor interpretation, and market reactions. The contribution is not simply to add Taiwan as another case of geopolitical risk. It is to explain why Taiwan's unresolved sovereignty status generates a recurring stream of politically significant events, why repeated exposure may alter investor expectations, and why markets price those events through competing interpretations of threat, opportunity, adaptation, and surprise. The framework therefore connects the structural condition of sovereignty ambiguity to observable financial outcomes.

This contribution is distinct from the existing literatures. Relative to sovereignty scholarship, the dissertation moves from recognition and status to market behaviour, showing how ambiguous sovereignty becomes economically consequential through recurring events. Relative to geopolitical-risk literature, it preserves the expectation that risk often has negative effects while specifying the interpretive mechanisms that condition those effects. Relative to geoeconomics and semiconductor politics, it shows that strategic importance is not merely a background feature of Taiwan's economy but a market-relevant signal that can generate both positive expectations and new constraints. The novelty lies in integrating these claims into a single framework rather than treating sovereignty, risk, economic statecraft, and investor behaviour as separate domains.

The contribution to International Relations is to connect sovereignty ambiguity and grey-zone pressure to observable financial-market behaviour. Rather than treating sovereignty as only a legal or diplomatic question, the dissertation asks how ambiguous sovereignty becomes priced through market reactions. The contribution to International Political Economy is to show how security, finance, economic statecraft, and semiconductor supply chains interact in a single case. Taiwan is not merely a geopolitical risk site. It is also a strategic economic node. Markets appear to respond to both dimensions.

The dissertation therefore advances a more conditional account of geopolitical risk. It does not reject the proposition that risk damages markets. Instead, it argues that this relationship is mediated by investor interpretation and shaped over time by adaptation. Repeated shocks may reduce novelty. Strategic-importance events may produce opportunity or constraint. Surprise may alter the magnitude of reaction. This framework is particularly suited to Taiwan, but it also has broader implications for other cases where political ambiguity, strategic competition, and economic centrality coexist.
