# Chapter 4: Research Design

## 4.1 Research Strategy

This dissertation uses an exploratory event-study research design to examine how Taiwan-related geopolitical shocks are associated with financial-market reactions. The research question asks how Taiwan-related geopolitical events affect markets through competing investor interpretations of threat, strategic importance, surprise, and adaptation. The purpose is not to prove that investors adapt to geopolitical risk. Rather, the objective is to evaluate whether observed market responses are consistent with an adaptation-based interpretation while taking rival explanations seriously.

The design follows from the theoretical framework developed in Chapter 3. Grey-zone sovereignty is treated as the upstream political condition that generates recurring Taiwan-related shocks. These shocks may be interpreted by investors as threats, as signs of strategic importance, as familiar repeated signals, or as surprises that change expectations. The empirical task is therefore to observe market reactions around identifiable Taiwan-related events and assess whether the direction and magnitude of abnormal returns align with the theoretical expectations. The event study is appropriate because the project is concerned with dated political events and short-run financial responses.

The analysis is descriptive and interpretive rather than causal in a strong econometric sense. Taiwan-related events rarely occur in isolation, and financial markets are affected by many simultaneous forces. For this reason, the dissertation does not claim that each event caused a particular return. Instead, it asks whether the pattern of market responses is more consistent with a simple geopolitical-risk model or with a more conditional model in which investor interpretation and adaptation mediate the effect of political shocks. This distinction is important. The empirical strategy can identify patterns that support, complicate, or weaken the theory. It cannot directly observe investor beliefs.

The research design combines three elements. First, it constructs a transparent event sample of Taiwan-related geopolitical and strategic-importance events. Second, it classifies events according to mechanism and category. Third, it calculates raw and benchmark-adjusted returns around event windows. The resulting evidence is used to compare risk events with strategic-importance events, to identify deviant cases, and to examine whether repeated grey-zone shocks appear to become less damaging over time.

## 4.2 Taiwan as a Critical Case

Taiwan is a critical case because it brings together sovereignty ambiguity, geopolitical risk, economic statecraft, and semiconductor centrality in unusually concentrated form. It possesses many practical attributes of sovereignty, including democratic institutions, independent policy capacity, territorial administration, and military capability. Yet its formal international recognition remains limited, and its status is continually contested by the People's Republic of China. This creates the condition identified in this dissertation as grey-zone sovereignty: a persistent political ambiguity that simultaneously generates recurring geopolitical risk and strategic economic importance.

Taiwan is critical for geopolitical-risk theory because conventional expectations would predict that cross-strait tension, military pressure, diplomatic confrontation, and coercive economic measures should place downward pressure on financial markets. Taiwan is not a peripheral security issue. It is one of the most visible flashpoints in U.S.-China relations. A serious escalation could disrupt trade, damage investor confidence, threaten supply chains, and create major regional-security consequences. If geopolitical risk should produce negative financial reactions anywhere, Taiwan is a plausible case in which to observe that relationship.

At the same time, Taiwan is critical for IPE and geoeconomics because it is not only a site of risk. It is also a strategic economic node. TSMC and Taiwan's semiconductor ecosystem sit at the centre of advanced chip manufacturing, AI infrastructure, and global technology competition. This means Taiwan-related events may communicate more than danger. They may also signal policy support, supply-chain protection, industrial subsidies, or the long-run value of strategic technologies. Taiwan therefore allows the dissertation to examine the tension between risk and strategic importance within the same case.

The case is especially useful for studying adaptation because Taiwan-related shocks recur. Military demonstrations, diplomatic protests, election-related warnings, sovereignty statements, U.S.-Taiwan interactions, export controls, and semiconductor-policy announcements are not one-off events. They are repeated manifestations of an unresolved sovereignty condition. This recurrence makes it possible to ask whether later shocks are interpreted differently from earlier shocks. Taiwan therefore functions as a critical case for testing whether markets respond only to objective risk, or whether repeated exposure changes the way risk is interpreted.

## 4.3 Event Selection

The event sample was constructed to include Taiwan-related events between 2022 and 2025 that could plausibly affect investor perceptions of Taiwan-related geopolitical risk or strategic economic importance. The inclusion criteria required that an event involve at least one of the following: Taiwan Strait military activity, Taiwan diplomatic relations, Taiwan-U.S. relations, China-Taiwan economic coercion, semiconductor geopolitics, export controls, AI supply-chain competition, or cross-strait crisis dynamics. The event also had to have a clearly identifiable date suitable for event-study analysis.

The first stage produced a broader candidate universe of Taiwan-related events. This included military exercises, diplomatic shocks, political signalling, economic coercion, export-control decisions, and strategic semiconductor events. Events were then reviewed for relevance to the research question. Routine business announcements, conferences, general China policy actions without a direct Taiwan connection, and corporate investment announcements without a clear geopolitical or strategic-importance component were excluded from the event-study sample. This exclusion rule is important because the dissertation studies market responses to politically meaningful shocks, not all news involving Taiwan or technology firms.

The selection process also addressed event-window contamination. Taiwan-related events often cluster. For example, the Pelosi crisis included prior warnings, the visit itself, trade restrictions, and PLA exercises within a narrow period. Treating each item as fully independent would overstate the amount of information in the sample and risk double-counting the same crisis episode. The cleaner sample therefore prioritises the event most likely to generate direct market repricing within severe overlap clusters. In the Pelosi cluster, the Pelosi Visit is treated as the preferred repricing event. In the Tsai-McCarthy and Joint Sword 2023 cluster, Joint Sword 2023 is preferred. In the Lai inauguration and Joint Sword-2024A cluster, Joint Sword-2024A is preferred. In the Lai National Day speech and Joint Sword-2024B cluster, Joint Sword-2024B is preferred.

The project therefore distinguishes between the broader calculated event set and the overlap-reduced event-study sample. The broader set is useful for descriptive exploration and for identifying patterns across more observations. The overlap-reduced sample is more appropriate for cautious interpretation because it reduces contamination across overlapping windows. This distinction reflects a central trade-off in the dissertation: increasing the number of events improves descriptive coverage, but reducing overlap improves interpretability.

Events with incomplete market data were excluded from estimation when the required event-day or event-window observations were unavailable. The final overlap-aware sample includes risk events and strategic-importance events, with risk events more numerous than strategic-importance events. This imbalance is substantively meaningful because Taiwan's grey-zone sovereignty produces more frequent military and diplomatic shocks than major semiconductor-policy shocks. It is also a limitation because it makes comparisons between mechanisms sensitive to a small number of strategic-importance observations.

## 4.4 Event Classification

Events are classified in two main ways: by event category and by mechanism. Event categories identify the substantive form of the event, such as Military Demonstration, Diplomatic Shock, Political Signalling, Economic Coercion, or Technology Competition. Mechanism identifies the theoretical interpretation most relevant to the event: Risk or Strategic_Importance.

Risk events are those that plausibly increase perceived geopolitical uncertainty around Taiwan. Military demonstrations are classified as risk events because they involve coercive signalling, operational pressure, or visible escalation in the Taiwan Strait. Diplomatic shocks are classified as risk events when they raise cross-strait tension, trigger PRC warnings, or challenge Taiwan's contested international status. Political-signalling events are classified as risk events when they involve sovereignty claims, independence warnings, or official statements likely to affect perceptions of conflict probability. Economic-coercion events are classified as risk events when they use trade or economic measures to punish or pressure Taiwan.

Strategic-importance events are those that highlight Taiwan's role in critical technologies, semiconductor supply chains, or U.S.-China technology competition. TSMC-related investment announcements, CHIPS Act developments, and major semiconductor export controls are classified as strategic-importance events because they signal Taiwan's central position in global technology politics. These events may generate positive market expectations if investors interpret them as evidence of state support, subsidies, industrial policy protection, or long-run demand for advanced chips. However, this classification does not assume a positive market reaction. BIS Advanced Semiconductor Export Controls are treated as a strategic-importance event because they concern semiconductor geopolitics, but they may be interpreted negatively if investors view them as restrictive, fragmenting, or costly.

The classification scheme therefore separates event type from expected outcome. Risk events are expected to be negative on average because threat, coercion, and escalation tend to increase uncertainty. Strategic-importance events are expected to be positive on average because strategic industries may attract investment, policy support, and supply-chain protection. Yet both expectations are probabilistic rather than deterministic. A risk event may generate muted or positive returns if investors interpret it as familiar, anticipated, or contained. A strategic-importance event may generate negative returns if it signals regulation, fragmentation, or constraints on market access.

This distinction is central to the dissertation's theoretical framework. Risk and strategic importance are not automatic market determinants. They are sources of investor interpretation. The classification identifies the type of information an event is likely to contain, while the event-study results show how markets appear to respond. Deviant cases are therefore theoretically important rather than merely inconvenient. Joint Sword-2024B is important because it is classified as a risk event yet produces positive abnormal returns in the current results. BIS Advanced Semiconductor Export Controls is important because it is classified as strategic importance yet produces negative abnormal returns. These cases help evaluate whether the adaptation and interpretation framework is more persuasive than a simple risk model.

## 4.5 Event Study Methodology

An event study examines how financial markets move around a specific event date. The basic logic is that if an event provides new information relevant to investors, prices may adjust around the date when that information becomes public. This approach is well suited to the dissertation because Taiwan-related geopolitical and strategic events can often be dated precisely. The Pelosi Visit, Joint Sword exercises, export controls, and TSMC investment announcements all have identifiable event dates.

The dissertation examines short event windows around each event. The main windows are Day 0, [-1,+1], [-3,+3], and [-7,+7]. Day 0 captures the immediate event-day reaction. The [-1,+1] window allows for small timing differences, such as events announced after market close or responses that occur across adjacent trading days. The [-3,+3] window captures somewhat broader short-run adjustment. The [-7,+7] window is the principal window for this dissertation because Taiwan-related events often unfold over several trading days, especially when diplomatic actions are followed by PRC military responses or official statements.

The analysis uses both raw returns and abnormal returns. Raw returns show how the TWSE and TSMC moved during the event window. Abnormal returns adjust these movements against broader market benchmarks. In the current design, TWSE returns are adjusted against NASDAQ returns, while TSMC returns are adjusted against the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index. The logic is straightforward: if the whole technology market rises or falls during the same period, raw Taiwan returns may exaggerate or understate the Taiwan-specific component. Benchmark adjustment therefore helps separate Taiwan-related movement from broader market movement, although it cannot fully solve the identification problem.

The abnormal returns used here are calculated through simple benchmark subtraction rather than a fully estimated market model. TWSE abnormal return is the TWSE daily return minus the NASDAQ daily return. TSMC abnormal return is the TSMC daily return minus the SOX daily return. Cumulative abnormal returns are then calculated by summing daily abnormal returns over the event window. This method is accessible, transparent, and appropriate for a preliminary dissertation-level event study. It also has limitations. It assumes that the benchmark captures relevant market-wide movement and does not estimate asset-specific beta or normal-return behaviour.

The event-study results should therefore be interpreted cautiously. A positive abnormal return does not prove that investors adapted, and a negative abnormal return does not prove that an event caused market damage. Rather, the results indicate whether observed movements are consistent with particular interpretations. If risk events are negative on average, this supports the threat expectation. If repeated risk events become less negative over time, this is consistent with adaptation, provided rival explanations are considered. If strategic-importance events are positive on average, this supports the opportunity expectation. The method provides evidence for evaluating theoretical consistency, not definitive causal proof.

## 4.6 Variables and Operationalisation

The dependent variable is financial-market reaction. It is operationalised through daily returns, Day 0 abnormal returns, and cumulative abnormal returns for TWSE and TSMC. The TWSE represents the broader Taiwan equity market and is used to capture market-wide Taiwan exposure. TSMC represents Taiwan's strategic semiconductor position and is used to capture firm-level exposure to technology competition, supply-chain politics, and AI-related expectations.

The main independent variable at the theoretical level is grey-zone sovereignty. Because grey-zone sovereignty is a structural condition rather than a daily numerical variable, it is operationalised through the event sample it generates. The observable events are the empirical manifestations of Taiwan's unresolved status and strategic economic centrality. In this sense, the event dataset is the bridge between the theoretical condition and the market outcomes.

The main event-level variables are mechanism, event category, event repetition, and surprise level. Mechanism distinguishes Risk from Strategic_Importance. Event category identifies the substantive form of the event. Event repetition measures the degree to which similar events have occurred previously, especially within repeated military-demonstration families. Surprise level measures the extent to which an event was anticipated before Day 0. The dissertation uses surprise cautiously because low surprise may indicate that a muted market reaction reflects anticipation rather than adaptation.

Adaptation is not directly observed. It is inferred from patterns consistent with learning and reduced novelty. The clearest theoretical expectation is that repeated grey-zone shocks should become less damaging over time if investors learn to interpret them as familiar or contained. This inference is strengthened when repeated events are comparable, when later events produce weaker negative abnormal returns, and when the pattern remains visible after benchmark adjustment. It is weakened if the same pattern can be explained by prior anticipation, AI-sector momentum, semiconductor-cycle strength, or benchmark choice.

The operationalisation therefore reflects the cautious logic of the dissertation. Investor interpretation is the immediate mechanism producing market reaction, while adaptation is the deeper mechanism that may change interpretation over time. The empirical analysis does not observe investors interpreting events in real time. It observes market prices and evaluates whether the observed patterns align with the theoretical expectations.

## 4.7 Rival Explanations

The first rival explanation is anticipation. Many Taiwan-related events are partly predictable. Military responses may follow diplomatic visits, inaugurations, speeches, or U.S.-Taiwan interactions. If investors expected a PRC response before Day 0, market adjustment may have occurred before the event window. A muted or positive event-window reaction would then reflect prior pricing rather than adaptation. This is the most serious rival explanation because repeated shocks are often easier to anticipate. The dissertation addresses this by using pre-event windows and by treating surprise coding as a necessary control rather than as evidence of adaptation.

The second rival explanation is the AI boom. The sample period overlaps with a major surge in investor enthusiasm around artificial intelligence, advanced chips, and high-performance computing. TSMC is closely connected to this ecosystem. Positive abnormal returns around TSMC or Taiwan technology events may therefore reflect AI-related optimism rather than geopolitical interpretation. This is particularly relevant for later events and for cases where TSMC outperforms the broader Taiwan market.

The third rival explanation is the semiconductor cycle. Semiconductor firms move through cycles of demand, inventory adjustment, capital expenditure, and earnings expectations. TSMC may outperform or underperform because of sector-specific conditions rather than because investors are interpreting Taiwan-related geopolitical events differently. The use of SOX as a benchmark partially addresses this issue, but it does not eliminate it. A stronger future design would compare TSMC with Samsung, SK Hynix, NVIDIA, and other relevant semiconductor peers.

The fourth rival explanation is benchmark specification. The current benchmark choices are defensible but not definitive. NASDAQ is a U.S. technology-heavy index rather than a Taiwan-specific counterfactual. SOX captures semiconductor-sector movement, but it may also be affected by the same AI and technology shocks that affect TSMC. Different benchmarks, such as MSCI Asia, MSCI Emerging Markets, regional technology indices, or firm-level peers, could change the size or sign of abnormal returns. This matters because the raw versus abnormal comparison already shows that adjustment method affects interpretation.

The fifth rival explanation is event-selection bias. The event sample is manually constructed and may overrepresent visible, headline-grabbing military and diplomatic events. Strategic-importance events are fewer in number, which makes their average returns sensitive to individual cases. Event clusters may also overweight particular crisis periods. The dissertation addresses this by documenting inclusion and exclusion rules, identifying overlap clusters, and distinguishing the broader calculated sample from the overlap-reduced sample. Nevertheless, event selection remains a significant limitation.

These rival explanations do not invalidate the research design. They define the conditions under which the adaptation interpretation should be treated cautiously. If repeated risk events become less negative even after accounting for anticipation, benchmarks, AI-sector movement, semiconductor cycles, and event selection, confidence in the adaptation interpretation would increase. If the pattern disappears under these checks, the adaptation argument would need to be revised.

## 4.8 Limitations

The first limitation is sample size. The final overlap-aware sample is necessarily small because Taiwan-related geopolitical shocks cluster and because the study requires complete market data around each event. This limits statistical inference and makes category averages sensitive to individual observations. The dissertation therefore uses the event study as a structured descriptive method rather than as a basis for strong causal claims.

The second limitation is event-window overlap. Taiwan-related crises often unfold over several days or weeks. Diplomatic visits may be preceded by warnings and followed by military exercises, sanctions, or trade restrictions. Even with overlap reduction, it is difficult to isolate a single information shock. The event-study windows should therefore be interpreted as capturing market reactions around episodes rather than perfectly isolated events.

The third limitation is market-data and calendar alignment. Taiwan and U.S. markets do not always trade on the same days, and event dates sometimes fall on weekends or holidays. The study uses the nearest available trading dates where necessary, but this introduces timing uncertainty. The [-7,+7] window helps reduce some of this problem, but it cannot eliminate it.

The fourth limitation is the abnormal-return model. Simple benchmark subtraction is transparent but limited. It does not estimate normal returns, beta, volatility, or asset-specific sensitivity to benchmarks. It also relies on the assumption that NASDAQ and SOX are appropriate controls. Future work should test alternative benchmarks and market-model specifications.

The fifth limitation is measurement of adaptation. Adaptation is inferred rather than directly observed. The dissertation does not have survey evidence, trading-desk data, analyst-expectation data, or direct measures of investor beliefs. It infers adaptation only when repeated shocks appear to become less damaging in a way that is consistent with the theory. This inference remains vulnerable to anticipation and broader market conditions.

The final limitation is scope. The dissertation focuses on TWSE and TSMC because they represent broad Taiwan equity exposure and strategic semiconductor exposure. This is theoretically justified, but it excludes other relevant assets such as USD/TWD, credit default swaps, options-implied volatility, sectoral indices, and foreign-exposed Taiwan firms. A fuller study would incorporate additional asset classes to examine whether adaptation appears across markets or only in equities.

Taken together, these limitations reinforce the dissertation's cautious empirical claim. The research design does not prove investor adaptation. It evaluates whether Taiwan-related market responses are consistent with an adaptation-based interpretation of grey-zone sovereignty. This is a more defensible and theoretically useful objective than claiming to identify a definitive causal mechanism from a small event-study sample.
