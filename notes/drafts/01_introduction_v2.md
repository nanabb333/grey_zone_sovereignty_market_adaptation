# Grey-Zone Sovereignty and Market Adaptation: Explaining Financial Responses to Taiwan-Related Geopolitical Shocks

Why do some Taiwan-related geopolitical shocks fail to generate persistent market damage despite elevated objective risk? This question sits at the center of the paper. Taiwan is one of the most visible geopolitical flashpoints in the global economy, and conventional geopolitical-risk theories would lead us to expect that military pressure, diplomatic confrontation, and cross-strait uncertainty should produce negative financial-market reactions. Yet preliminary event-study evidence does not consistently support this expectation. Some Taiwan-related shocks, such as the Pelosi Visit in August 2022 and Joint Sword 2023, are associated with negative abnormal returns. Other events complicate the picture. Joint Sword-2024B, despite being a military demonstration, is associated with positive abnormal returns in the current event window. TSMC-related CHIPS Act and U.S. expansion events are also associated with positive abnormal returns. Risk events are negative on average, but with important exceptions. Strategic Importance events are positive on average, but they are not uniformly positive. The puzzle is not whether geopolitical risk matters. It is why market reactions vary so sharply across events that all emerge from Taiwan's contested geopolitical position.

This paper argues that the answer lies in Taiwan's condition of grey-zone sovereignty. I define grey-zone sovereignty as a persistent condition of political ambiguity that simultaneously generates recurring geopolitical risk and strategic economic importance. The concept does not primarily ask whether Taiwan should be classified as a state in formal legal terms. Instead, it functions as an explanatory framework for market behavior. Taiwan possesses many practical attributes of sovereignty, including self-government, democratic institutions, military capacity, independent economic policy, and deep integration into global trade and finance. At the same time, it lacks broad formal diplomatic recognition and remains embedded within U.S.-China strategic competition. This ambiguity produces recurring crisis signals, but it also reinforces Taiwan's strategic value because Taiwan sits at the center of advanced semiconductor production, artificial intelligence infrastructure, and global technology competition.

Grey-zone sovereignty therefore creates a distinctive market problem. Taiwan is not only a site of geopolitical risk; it is also a strategic economic node. Its political ambiguity exposes markets to military exercises, diplomatic shocks, coercive economic measures, and sovereignty-related political signaling. Its economic centrality, especially through Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company, makes Taiwan indispensable to supply chains that are central to U.S.-China competition. As a result, Taiwan-related events can carry multiple meanings for investors. A diplomatic visit may be interpreted as a crisis trigger. A military exercise may signal escalation risk. A semiconductor policy announcement may signal state support, supply-chain resilience, or long-run strategic value. The same grey-zone condition that makes Taiwan vulnerable can also make it valuable.

The empirical puzzle emerges because these two forces do not translate automatically into market outcomes. If geopolitical risk alone determined financial reactions, higher-risk events should consistently produce negative abnormal returns. If strategic importance alone determined reactions, technology and semiconductor events should consistently produce positive abnormal returns. The preliminary results show neither pattern. The Pelosi Visit and Joint Sword 2023 fit the conventional risk expectation, but Joint Sword-2024B does not. TSMC CHIPS Act events fit the strategic-importance expectation, but BIS Advanced Semiconductor Export Controls does not. The BIS case is especially important because it is clearly connected to semiconductor geopolitics and strategic technology competition, yet it appears among the most negative observations in the current results. This suggests that Risk and Strategic Importance are not automatic determinants of market outcomes. They are sources of interpretation.

The revised theoretical mechanism developed in this paper places investor interpretation between geopolitical events and market reactions. The framework is:

```text
Grey-Zone Sovereignty
        ↓
Geopolitical Event
        ↓
Investor Interpretation
        ├── Threat
        ├── Opportunity
        ├── Adaptation
        └── Surprise
        ↓
Market Reaction
```

In this framework, grey-zone sovereignty generates the conditions under which Taiwan-related geopolitical events occur. These events then require interpretation. Threat refers to the perception that an event raises the probability of coercion, escalation, disruption, or conflict. Opportunity refers to the perception that an event highlights Taiwan's strategic value, policy support, or technological centrality. Adaptation refers to the possibility that repeated grey-zone events become more familiar over time, reducing the intensity of market reactions even when objective risk remains high. Surprise refers to the extent to which an event differs from prior expectations. A high-risk event that is widely anticipated may generate a weaker reaction than a lower-risk event that unexpectedly changes investor beliefs.

This interpretation-centered framework helps explain why Taiwan-related shocks produce heterogeneous financial responses. A military event may generate negative abnormal returns if investors interpret it as a new escalation. The same kind of event may generate a muted or even positive reaction if it is interpreted as a familiar grey-zone demonstration that does not alter expected fundamentals. A semiconductor event may generate positive abnormal returns if investors interpret it as policy support or supply-chain strengthening. The same broad category of technology event may generate negative abnormal returns if investors interpret it as a constraint, disruption, or sign of broader U.S.-China technological fragmentation. The market response is therefore not a direct mechanical result of event type. It depends on how investors interpret the event in relation to prior expectations, sector conditions, and Taiwan's strategic position.

This argument does not deny the importance of geopolitical risk. Rather, it reframes risk as one component of a broader interpretive process. Taiwan's grey-zone sovereignty produces ambiguous market signals because it combines vulnerability and indispensability. The vulnerability side is visible in cross-strait military pressure, diplomatic conflict, and coercive economic action. The indispensability side is visible in semiconductor supply chains, U.S. industrial policy, AI infrastructure, and the strategic value of TSMC. Financial markets may respond to either side, and sometimes to both at once. This is why a simple Risk-to-Market Reaction model is insufficient.

Methodologically, the paper uses an event-study design to examine Taiwan-related geopolitical events from 2022 through 2025. The analysis focuses on Taiwan's broader equity market, represented by the TWSE/TWSE Index, and on TSMC as a firm-level indicator of Taiwan's semiconductor and strategic-technology exposure. The event sample includes diplomatic shocks, military demonstrations, political signaling, economic coercion, export-control events, and semiconductor-policy announcements. Rather than relying only on raw returns, the project calculates benchmark-adjusted abnormal returns. TWSE returns are adjusted against a broader technology-market benchmark, while TSMC returns are adjusted against a semiconductor-sector benchmark. The analysis examines event-day abnormal returns and cumulative abnormal returns over short event windows.

The event-study design is appropriate because the paper is concerned with how markets react around identifiable geopolitical events. However, the method is used cautiously. The analysis does not claim that each event caused a specific market movement. Event studies are vulnerable to anticipation, overlapping event windows, benchmark choice, small sample size, and broader market conditions. These limitations are especially important in the Taiwan case, where political events often cluster around diplomatic visits, elections, speeches, and military responses. The purpose of the empirical analysis is therefore not to produce final causal estimates. It is to assess whether observed abnormal-return patterns are consistent with a simple geopolitical-risk model or whether they point toward a more complex interpretation-centered framework.

The current evidence supports the need for such a framework. Risk events are negative on average for both TWSE and TSMC, but several Risk events produce positive abnormal returns. Strategic Importance events are positive on average, but the BIS export-control case shows that technology-related events can also be negative. TWSE and TSMC sometimes diverge, suggesting that broad Taiwan market risk and semiconductor-specific expectations do not always move together. The pattern is especially important for understanding market adaptation. In the limited sequence from Pelosi Visit to Joint Sword-2024B, later repeated military demonstrations appear less consistently damaging than the earlier crisis period. This does not prove adaptation, but it suggests that repetition and surprise should be included in the theoretical model.

The paper makes three contributions. First, it connects grey-zone sovereignty theory with financial-market behavior. Existing discussions of Taiwan's status often emphasize recognition, deterrence, military coercion, or great-power competition. This project extends that discussion by asking how financial markets interpret events that arise from Taiwan's ambiguous status. In doing so, it links International Relations, International Political Economy, event-study methods, and computational social science.

Second, the paper shows that geopolitical risk does not uniformly correspond to negative abnormal returns. The Taiwan case is especially useful because it combines visible military and diplomatic shocks with strategic economic value. The findings indicate that market reactions vary across event types, mechanisms, and assets. Risk events tend to be negative on average, but some Risk events are positive. Strategic Importance events tend to be positive on average, but some technology-related events are negative. This variation is theoretically important because it suggests that objective risk alone is insufficient to explain financial responses.

Third, the paper develops a framework linking geopolitical shocks, investor interpretation, and market adaptation. The framework suggests that Taiwan-related events should be analyzed not only by their objective severity but also by how investors interpret them. Threat, opportunity, adaptation, and surprise provide a more flexible set of mechanisms for explaining why similar event types may generate different market reactions. This framework also creates a basis for future empirical work on event repetition, anticipation, event families, benchmark sensitivity, and asset-specific responses.

The broader significance of the project lies in its treatment of Taiwan as both a geopolitical flashpoint and a strategic economic node. In public discourse, Taiwan is often framed either as a security problem or as a semiconductor powerhouse. Grey-zone sovereignty requires holding both dimensions together. Taiwan's political ambiguity creates risk, but its economic centrality creates value. Markets appear to respond to both, though not always in predictable ways. This duality helps explain why Taiwan-related shocks do not always generate the persistent market damage that conventional geopolitical-risk expectations would predict.

The remainder of the paper develops this argument in stages. It first situates Taiwan's grey-zone sovereignty within debates on recognition, strategic ambiguity, and U.S.-China competition, while treating the concept primarily as a framework for market behavior rather than as a legal-status debate. It then describes the event-study design and the construction of the Taiwan-related event sample. The empirical sections present abnormal-return patterns for TWSE and TSMC, comparing Risk and Strategic Importance events across event windows. The discussion evaluates market adaptation, competing explanations, and the limits of the current design. The paper concludes by proposing a revised research agenda for studying how financial markets interpret geopolitical shocks in cases where sovereignty, security, and strategic economic value are deeply intertwined.
