# Event Coding Rules

## Assignment Protocol

When assigning `risk_level` or `strategic_value`:

1. Never guess silently.
2. Provide a short justification.
3. If uncertain, flag the event for manual review.
4. Return these fields:
   - `event_name`
   - `risk_level`
   - `strategic_value`
   - `reasoning`

Recommended output format:

| event_name | risk_level | strategic_value | reasoning |
| --- | ---: | ---: | --- |
| Example Event | 3 | 4 | Brief explanation of why the event receives these scores. |

## `risk_level`

Definition:
The estimated geopolitical risk generated by an event.

Coding Scheme:

### 1 = Very Low

Routine event with little geopolitical significance.

Examples:
- Normal trade meeting
- Routine diplomatic statement

### 2 = Low

Minor political signal with limited market impact.

Examples:
- Congressional statement
- Small policy announcement

### 3 = Medium

Noticeable increase in geopolitical uncertainty.

Examples:
- Taiwan election
- High-profile diplomatic visit

### 4 = High

Major escalation in cross-strait tensions.

Examples:
- Large PLA military exercise
- Significant sanctions announcement

### 5 = Very High

Potential crisis or conflict scenario.

Examples:
- Missile launch near Taiwan
- Military blockade
- Direct military confrontation

---

## `strategic_value`

Definition:
The degree to which an event highlights Taiwan's strategic importance in global technology, security, or supply chains.

Coding Scheme:

### 1 = Very Low

Little connection to Taiwan's strategic role.

### 2 = Low

Limited relevance.

### 3 = Medium

Moderate relevance to Taiwan's strategic position.

### 4 = High

Strong relevance to semiconductor, AI, or security issues.

### 5 = Very High

Directly linked to Taiwan's critical role in global supply chains or U.S.-China competition.

Examples:
- TSMC expansion
- Nvidia AI investment
- Advanced semiconductor cooperation
- Major U.S.-Taiwan technology agreement
