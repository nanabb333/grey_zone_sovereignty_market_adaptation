# Revision Summary

## 1. What Was Changed

The revised introduction was saved as `01_introduction_v2.md`. The original `01_introduction_v1.md` was preserved.

Main changes:

- Moved the central puzzle into the opening paragraph.
- Reframed grey-zone sovereignty as a market-facing explanatory concept.
- Added the explicit definition: "a persistent condition of political ambiguity that simultaneously generates recurring geopolitical risk and strategic economic importance."
- Clarified that the paper is not primarily about whether Taiwan is a state in legal terms.
- Strengthened the mechanism from a direct risk/strategic-importance model to an investor-interpretation model.
- Added the revised mechanism: Grey-Zone Sovereignty -> Geopolitical Event -> Investor Interpretation -> Market Reaction.
- Made `Threat`, `Opportunity`, `Adaptation`, and `Surprise` central to the theoretical framework.
- Used BIS Advanced Semiconductor Export Controls as a motivating exception showing that Strategic Importance events are not always positive.
- Re-emphasized that Risk and Strategic Importance are sources of interpretation, not automatic determinants of market outcomes.

## 2. Why It Was Changed

The first version introduced Taiwan's ambiguous status clearly, but it leaned too much toward a general discussion of sovereignty attributes and limited diplomatic recognition. That framing was accurate but did not fully explain why the concept matters for financial-market behavior.

The revision makes the introduction more puzzle-driven. Instead of beginning mainly with theory, it begins with the empirical problem: some Taiwan-related geopolitical shocks fail to generate persistent market damage despite elevated objective risk.

The theoretical discussion was revised because the results do not support a simple pathway from geopolitical risk to negative returns or from strategic importance to positive returns. The empirical exceptions require a framework in which investors interpret events through multiple channels.

## 3. How the Revised Version Better Matches the Empirical Findings

The revised version better matches the findings in three ways.

First, it explains why Risk events can be negative on average but still include positive cases such as Joint Sword-2024B. Under the revised framework, military events may be interpreted as threats, but they may also be interpreted as repeated, anticipated, or manageable grey-zone behavior.

Second, it explains why Strategic Importance events can be positive on average but still include negative cases such as BIS Advanced Semiconductor Export Controls. Under the revised framework, technology events may signal opportunity, but they may also signal constraint, disruption, or uncertainty.

Third, it links the empirical variation directly to investor interpretation. The revised introduction no longer treats Risk and Strategic Importance as automatic market determinants. Instead, they become inputs into interpretation, filtered through threat, opportunity, adaptation, and surprise.

This makes the paper's logic better aligned with the event-study evidence and with the revised research question.
