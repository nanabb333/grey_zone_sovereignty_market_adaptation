# Literature Architecture V1

## Purpose

This chapter does not provide a traditional literature review. It builds the intellectual architecture of the paper by identifying the literatures that make the research puzzle visible, specifying what each literature explains, and clarifying what each literature cannot explain on its own.

The central puzzle is: why do some Taiwan-related geopolitical shocks fail to generate persistent market damage despite elevated objective risk?

The project sits at the intersection of five literatures: grey-zone sovereignty, geopolitical risk, international political economy, strategic importance and semiconductor geopolitics, and market interpretation and adaptation. Each literature contributes something necessary. None is sufficient by itself. The paper's contribution emerges from combining them into a framework in which Taiwan's political ambiguity generates both risk and strategic importance, but market outcomes depend on investor interpretation.

## 1. Grey-Zone Sovereignty

### Core Argument

The grey-zone sovereignty literature provides the political foundation of the project. It begins from the observation that Taiwan does not fit neatly into conventional categories of sovereignty. Taiwan possesses many practical attributes of sovereignty: self-government, democratic institutions, military capacity, territorial administration, and independent economic policy. Yet it lacks broad formal diplomatic recognition and remains structurally constrained by the People's Republic of China's sovereignty claim and by U.S.-China strategic competition.

Constructivist approaches to sovereignty, associated with writers such as Wendt, are useful because they treat sovereignty not only as a legal status but also as a social and political relationship shaped by recognition, interaction, and shared expectations. Taiwan sovereignty scholarship, including work associated with Shen, Chiang, and broader Taiwan statehood debates, explains why Taiwan's position is neither simply normal statehood nor simple non-statehood. Taiwan exists in a persistent condition of ambiguity.

For this project, grey-zone sovereignty is defined as a persistent condition of political ambiguity that simultaneously generates recurring geopolitical risk and strategic economic importance. This definition moves the concept away from a narrow legal debate over whether Taiwan is a state. The point is not to resolve Taiwan's status. The point is to explain why Taiwan's ambiguous status produces recurring political shocks that financial markets must interpret.

### What It Explains

This literature explains why Taiwan-related events are unusually politically charged. Diplomatic visits, presidential elections, military exercises, recognition shifts, and policy statements often matter because they occur within an unresolved sovereignty environment. A similar diplomatic event involving a fully recognized state may not produce the same level of cross-strait tension. In Taiwan's case, the ambiguity of status turns otherwise routine political signals into sovereignty-relevant events.

Grey-zone sovereignty also explains why Taiwan-related shocks recur. The ambiguity is not temporary. It produces repeated cycles of signaling, coercion, reassurance, and counter-signaling. China may use military demonstrations to contest Taiwan's political autonomy. Taiwan may use democratic elections and diplomatic engagement to reinforce self-government. The United States may use strategic ambiguity, arms sales, visits, and technology policy to support Taiwan without fully resolving its status.

### What It Cannot Explain

On its own, the sovereignty literature does not explain financial-market variation. It can tell us why Taiwan is politically ambiguous and why cross-strait events recur, but it does not predict why one military demonstration is followed by negative abnormal returns while another is followed by positive abnormal returns. It also does not explain why some technology-related events appear positive while export-control events can be negative.

The sovereignty literature often focuses on recognition, statehood, identity, legal status, and strategic ambiguity. These are essential, but they do not directly specify how investors process Taiwan-related events. Without an investor-interpretation mechanism, grey-zone sovereignty risks becoming background context rather than an explanation of market behavior.

### Relevance to Taiwan

Taiwan is the central case for this framework because it combines autonomy, contested recognition, military vulnerability, and strategic economic importance. It is not merely a disputed territory, and it is not simply a normal sovereign state. It is a politically autonomous entity whose status is continuously negotiated through cross-strait relations, U.S.-China competition, and global economic dependence on Taiwanese technology.

### Relevance to This Project

This project uses grey-zone sovereignty as the upstream condition that makes Taiwan-related geopolitical shocks recurrent and market-relevant. It explains why events such as the Pelosi Visit, Joint Sword exercises, and sovereignty-related speeches can become financial events. However, the project extends the literature by asking how markets interpret those events, and why the same sovereignty condition can produce both negative risk signals and positive strategic-value signals.

## 2. Geopolitical Risk

### Core Argument

The geopolitical-risk literature explains how political conflict, military tension, terrorism, sanctions, and interstate rivalry can affect markets. Work such as Caldara and Iacoviello's geopolitical risk framework treats geopolitical risk as a measurable source of uncertainty that can affect investment, asset prices, volatility, and macroeconomic expectations. Political risk and event-study literatures similarly assume that events which raise uncertainty should generally produce negative financial responses.

The intuition is straightforward. Investors dislike uncertainty. Military escalation, sanctions, diplomatic rupture, and conflict risk can reduce expected cash flows, increase discount rates, disrupt trade, or raise the probability of future crisis. A simple geopolitical-risk model therefore predicts that higher objective risk should generate more negative market reactions.

### What It Explains

This literature explains why the Pelosi Visit and Joint Sword 2023 generated negative abnormal returns in the current project. Both events fit the standard risk logic: visible diplomatic or military escalation increased uncertainty around Taiwan, and markets responded negatively after benchmark adjustment. It also explains why the average Risk event in the sample is negative for both TWSE and TSMC.

The geopolitical-risk literature also justifies event-study methods. If political shocks change investor expectations, returns around the event window can provide evidence about how markets price that information. The event-study approach is therefore well suited to the paper's dependent variable: abnormal returns around identifiable Taiwan-related shocks.

### What It Cannot Explain

The problem is that the simple risk model does not explain the exceptions. Joint Sword-2024B is a military demonstration but appears among the most positive observations. Some Risk events have positive average cumulative abnormal returns. Meanwhile, BIS Advanced Semiconductor Export Controls is connected to strategic technology competition but appears among the most negative observations.

This means the geopolitical-risk literature is necessary but incomplete. It explains why risk should matter, but it often assumes that risk translates into negative market outcomes too directly. Taiwan presents a challenge because risk is not the only information contained in Taiwan-related events. Some events also signal strategic importance, policy support, supply-chain restructuring, or investor adaptation to repeated grey-zone behavior.

### Relevance to Taiwan

Taiwan is a hard case for geopolitical-risk theory. It is one of the clearest sites of military and diplomatic risk in the global economy, but it is also central to the semiconductor supply chain. A Taiwan event can simultaneously signal danger and indispensability. This makes Taiwan an ideal case for testing whether geopolitical risk always has the expected negative market effect.

### Relevance to This Project

The project begins with the expectation supplied by the geopolitical-risk literature: higher risk should produce negative abnormal returns. It then uses Taiwan to show why that expectation needs refinement. The paper does not reject geopolitical-risk theory. It adds a conditioning layer: geopolitical risk affects markets through investor interpretation, and that interpretation can be shaped by surprise, repetition, strategic importance, and asset-specific exposure.

## 3. International Political Economy

### Core Argument

International Political Economy explains how economic power, financial markets, statecraft, and strategic competition interact. Baldwin's work on economic statecraft is relevant because it treats economic tools as instruments of political power. Helleiner and Eichengreen help locate monetary and financial power within international hierarchy. Financial statecraft literature more broadly shows that sanctions, capital flows, investment policy, export controls, and market access can become tools of geopolitical competition.

This literature shifts attention from military danger alone to the political uses of economic interdependence. It asks how markets respond when states weaponize trade, technology, finance, and supply chains.

### What It Explains

IPE explains why Taiwan-related events are not only security events. They are also economic and financial events. China's coercive trade measures after the Pelosi Visit, U.S. export controls on advanced semiconductors, CHIPS Act subsidies, and TSMC overseas expansion all sit at the intersection of market behavior and strategic competition.

It also explains why benchmark-adjusted returns are theoretically meaningful. Markets do not respond only to local politics. They process global financial conditions, technology competition, sanctions risk, and expectations about future state support. Taiwan's financial markets are embedded in an international political economy of supply chains, export controls, and industrial policy.

### What It Cannot Explain

IPE alone does not fully explain why similar forms of economic statecraft produce different market reactions. Export controls may be interpreted as strategic containment, supply-chain disruption, or long-run protection of advanced technology. CHIPS Act support may be interpreted as positive state backing or as evidence of geopolitical fragmentation. The same economic-policy field can generate both opportunity and constraint.

This literature can explain why markets and state power interact, but it does not automatically specify the direction of the market reaction. It also tends to operate at a broad structural level, while this project needs event-level interpretation.

### Relevance to Taiwan

Taiwan is a core IPE case because its strategic value is inseparable from its economic position. Taiwan's semiconductor sector is a site where private firms, state policy, export controls, and military security intersect. Taiwan's market reactions cannot be understood only as reactions to cross-strait risk. They are also reactions to the changing organization of global economic power.

### Relevance to This Project

IPE provides the bridge between geopolitical shocks and financial-market outcomes. It explains why export controls, sanctions, TSMC investment, and supply-chain restructuring belong in the same research design as military demonstrations and diplomatic shocks. The project contributes to IPE by showing that markets may price strategic competition through multiple interpretive channels rather than through a single risk premium.

## 4. Strategic Importance and Semiconductor Geopolitics

### Core Argument

The literature on Taiwan's strategic importance and semiconductor geopolitics explains why Taiwan matters beyond the sovereignty dispute itself. Work such as Bellocchi's strategic-importance framing, CHIPS Act analysis, semiconductor supply-chain research, and technology-competition literature emphasizes Taiwan's central role in advanced manufacturing, AI infrastructure, and U.S.-China technological rivalry.

This literature argues that Taiwan is valuable because it occupies a crucial position in the production of advanced semiconductors. TSMC is not simply a domestic company. It is a global strategic asset. Semiconductor capacity, advanced-node production, and supply-chain resilience have become matters of national security and industrial policy.

### What It Explains

This literature explains why some Taiwan-related events generate positive abnormal returns. TSMC Arizona expansion, TSMC CHIPS Act preliminary terms, and TSMC U.S. expansion events may be interpreted by investors as signals of policy support, strategic investment, and long-run supply-chain value. The positive average returns for Strategic_Importance events fit this logic.

It also explains why TSMC may react differently from the broader Taiwan market. TWSE may capture broad market vulnerability, while TSMC carries a more specific exposure to semiconductor demand, global technology policy, AI supply chains, and strategic investment.

### What It Cannot Explain

Strategic-importance literature can overcorrect the risk model if it assumes that semiconductor importance is always positive for markets. The BIS Advanced Semiconductor Export Controls case complicates this view. It is clearly connected to semiconductor geopolitics, but it appears among the most negative events in the current results. Strategic importance can signal opportunity, but it can also signal restriction, fragmentation, compliance costs, or supply-chain uncertainty.

The literature also does not explain why some military events produce positive abnormal returns. Strategic importance helps explain why Taiwan has value, but not why markets may adapt to repeated military demonstrations.

### Relevance to Taiwan

Taiwan's strategic importance is central to its contemporary geopolitical position. Its semiconductor sector affects how the United States, China, Japan, Europe, and global firms think about security and supply-chain resilience. Taiwan's value cannot be separated from its role in advanced technology.

### Relevance to This Project

This literature supplies the second force in the project's framework: Strategic_Importance. It helps explain why grey-zone sovereignty can produce market opportunity as well as risk. The project contributes by showing that strategic importance should not be treated as automatically positive. It must be interpreted by investors in relation to policy support, export-control constraints, sector conditions, and surprise.

## 5. Market Interpretation and Adaptation

### Core Argument

Market interpretation and adaptation is the most important literature architecture for this project because it explains why similar events can generate different reactions. This body of work includes expectation formation, surprise, repeated shocks, market learning, investor attention, and event-study approaches to anticipated versus unexpected information.

The core argument is that markets do not react mechanically to objective events. They react to events relative to expectations. An event that is severe but anticipated may have a limited Day 0 reaction. An event that is moderate but surprising may have a larger effect. Repeated shocks may become familiar, producing adaptation or desensitization. Investors may also learn to distinguish between symbolic signaling and material escalation.

### What It Explains

This literature directly addresses the central puzzle. It explains why the Pelosi Visit and early crisis events may generate negative returns while later military demonstrations may be less damaging. It also explains why Joint Sword-2024B can be a Risk event but still produce positive abnormal returns: investors may have interpreted it as a repeated, anticipated, or manageable grey-zone demonstration rather than a new crisis.

It also helps explain the role of surprise. A military exercise after a predictable political trigger may be less informative than a sudden or unexpected escalation. Similarly, a technology-policy announcement may generate different reactions depending on whether it signals expected support, unexpected restriction, or deeper strategic fragmentation.

### What It Cannot Explain

Market-interpretation literature can explain variation in reaction, but it does not by itself explain why Taiwan produces these repeated ambiguous events. For that, the project still needs grey-zone sovereignty. It also does not specify why some interpretations are about threat while others are about strategic opportunity. For that, the project needs geopolitical risk, IPE, and semiconductor geopolitics.

Adaptation is also difficult to measure. The current project has only limited event-repetition coding. Without careful coding of surprise, repetition, prior expectations, and event families, adaptation can become a plausible but underidentified explanation.

### Relevance to Taiwan

Taiwan is a strong case for market interpretation because many Taiwan-related shocks are recurrent. Military demonstrations, diplomatic protests, and sovereignty-related signaling do not occur only once. Investors may learn over time which signals are likely to alter fundamentals and which belong to a repeated pattern of grey-zone pressure.

### Relevance to This Project

This is the literature that turns the project from a simple risk study into an interpretation-centered event study. It explains why Risk and Strategic_Importance should be treated as sources of interpretation rather than automatic determinants of outcomes. The project's revised mechanism depends on this logic:

```text
Grey-Zone Sovereignty
        ↓
Geopolitical Event
        ↓
Investor Interpretation
        ├── Threat
        ├── Opportunity
        ├── Adaptation
        └── Surprise
        ↓
Market Reaction
```

The project's most important contribution is located here. It uses Taiwan to show that geopolitical shocks do not enter markets as simple risk inputs. They are interpreted through expectations, repetition, and strategic context.

## Literature Gap

The gap is not that nobody has studied Taiwan. Taiwan is widely studied in work on sovereignty, recognition, cross-strait relations, deterrence, U.S.-China competition, and semiconductor geopolitics. The gap is also not simply that financial markets have ignored geopolitics. Geopolitical-risk research and event-study methods already examine how political shocks affect asset prices.

The gap is that existing literatures generally assume, either explicitly or implicitly, that geopolitical risk translates into negative market outcomes. The sovereignty literature explains why Taiwan's status is ambiguous, but not how investors price that ambiguity. Geopolitical-risk literature explains why risk should hurt markets, but not why high-risk Taiwan events sometimes fail to generate persistent damage. IPE explains the interaction of markets and state power, but not the direction of event-level market interpretation. Semiconductor geopolitics explains Taiwan's strategic value, but not why some technology events are negative. Market-adaptation literature explains expectation-based variation, but not why Taiwan produces recurring ambiguous events in the first place.

This project fills the gap by combining these literatures into a single architecture. Grey-zone sovereignty provides the structural condition. Geopolitical risk and strategic importance provide competing sources of market interpretation. IPE explains why economic and financial instruments are part of strategic competition. Semiconductor geopolitics explains why Taiwan can be interpreted as an opportunity as well as a vulnerability. Market interpretation and adaptation explain why event effects vary across time, assets, and expectations.

The project's contribution is therefore not a claim that Taiwan is unique in a descriptive sense. It is a theoretical claim that Taiwan's grey-zone sovereignty creates a dual market condition: recurring geopolitical risk and strategic economic importance coexist. Market reactions depend on whether investors interpret a given event as threat, opportunity, adaptation, or surprise. This framework explains why Risk events can be negative on average but not uniformly negative, and why Strategic_Importance events can be positive on average but not uniformly positive.

This architecture positions the paper within IR and IPE. For IR, it connects sovereignty ambiguity and grey-zone pressure to observable market behavior. For IPE, it connects strategic competition, semiconductor supply chains, and financial pricing. For event-study research, it suggests that geopolitical shocks should be modeled not only by event severity but also by investor interpretation and repetition. The paper's empirical findings are preliminary, but they motivate a conceptual refinement: geopolitical risk does not automatically become market damage. It becomes market-relevant through interpretation.
